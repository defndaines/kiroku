## Fiction

If reading anthropology PhD theses is your jam, then I have the book for you. “Always Coming Home” is a stand-alone book, set in a distant future in what is now northern California. It mixes folk tales, novel “excerpts”, poetry, songs, and nonfiction-like exegesis. It reads slow, and although there is one character we come back to across several stories, there isn’t a straightforward plot here. It was interesting reading this shortly after “We Survived the Night”, where coyote is a masculine archetype; Coyote here is feminine. This future world has stepped back from most technology, and people are aware of the world but usually happy enough to stay among their community. It’s a twist on the utopian possibilities touched on in “The Dispossessed”. There are some real gems in here (“You don’t make peace, it just is”), but they do feel like a needle in a haystack for how long the book is. I don’t know who I’d recommend this to besides Le Guin completionists.

## Nonfiction

“Indignity” is marked as nonfiction, but blurs the line, much like “Autobiography of Cotton”, which I read recently. Some parts are pure memoir of the author visiting the archives in Albania, while others are direct translations of documents she uncovered. Mixed into this is a dramatized retelling of the early years of the author’s grandmother’s life. We learn about the transitions in Albania in the first half of the twentieth century, but not at the level you’d get from a history. There were parts of the book that dragged for me, though I stayed engaged and interested enough to see it through to the end.

## Graphic Novels

I first heard about “Supergirl: Woman of Tomorrow” after the “Superman” movie last year, when word started spreading that it was going to be the basis for the upcoming Supergirl movie. Kara is visiting a planet with a red sun when she encounters a girl, Ruthye, looking to avenge her father’s death. They set off across the universe to track down Krem of the Yellow Hills. The art style is detailed and mildly Art Nouveau in a way that reminds me of P. Craig Russell. The real punch of the story is in the last couple issues, and the middle can feel a little like filler until things coalesce.

“Elise and the New Partisans” is the fictionalized autobiography of Dominique Grange. After some success as a singer in 1960s France, Elise (Dominique) is more and more drawn to the causes of working people and other oppressed. With May ’68, there is no going back, and she devotes herself to Maoism, whether through factory work or more adventurous efforts. We see the brutality of the police against anyone who dares resist French capital, regardless of whether your ancestors called Algeria or France home. This is a pretty good history of 1960s and 1970s France. Although I’m sure some details are withheld, this does feel quite honest about her life and the events that shaped it. Definitely recommended.

After having discovered “Macanudo” earlier this year, I was definitely on-board when I saw a new volume come out. “The Way of the Penguin” continues the adventures of the characters well established by the artist, with brief tales of youth, tenderness, and discovery. There isn’t really a sequential nature to these strips, so you can pick up any volume you wish to get started.
