## Fiction

This book made a lot of shortlists, so I was expecting a solid read. “Flashlight” follows the lives of a family up to the 2000s. Serk was born in Japan to Korean parents during the Pacific War. Anne has a child out of wedlock, Tobias, who she gives up. The two meet, and Louisa is born in 1968. Ten years later, the family is shattered when Serk is presumed drown off the coast of Japan, Louisa washed ashore. The author is an excellent writer, and even though none of the primary characters are likable, I still felt compelled to read on. I did guess the twist from the beginning, but this is probably due to my knowledge of Japan and its history. I don’t know that I’d recommend this, though I’d be willing to read more of the author’s work.

This book seems to have won all the sci-fi awards the year it came out. “Ancillary Justice” bounces chapters between the “current” time and 1000 years before. Breq is an A.I., so has lived for 2000 years. She’s searching for something on a remote ice planet. Along the way, she stumbles upon a figure from her past. In a distant future, we get a great mystery which uses and explores several sci-fi concepts. You aren’t even sure if the protagonist is meant to be a hero as you begin. I enjoyed this and can see why it has gotten such high praise. I will try to read more of the series when time allows.

## Short Fiction

This novelette is short-listed for the 2026 Hugo Awards. “When He Calls Your Name” is a supernatural horror, of sorts, where our protagonist has had her husband lured away by a beautiful woman. The brazenness of it would floor anyone, but she is determined to get her husband back. This is a fun read, and it is centered around a gimmick that I thought was used well. That being said, it isn’t the best of the nominees.

This novelette is short-listed for the 2026 Hugo Awards. “The Millay Illusion” is set during they hey-day of traveling shows. The narrator grew up in a family working the circuit, and she sets out on her own as an opening act doing a mentalist performance. She then encounters Susanna, an illusionist of great talent but restricted by her gender. While most of Pinsker’s work is fantasy, this reads like historical fiction. Honestly, reading this as “fantasy” takes away from the mystery near the end of the story—it would be better to presume it was not supernatural. I liked the story, but not the strongest of the nominees.

## Manga

I finished the final two volumes of “Akira” this week. Characters spin into a final conflict, as Tetsuo’s power grows. I appreciate how the story ends. While a classic, I also could not see myself re-reading this.

## Graphic Novels

I picked up “Pizza Witch” because it looked like it would be silly and fun. I wasn’t disappointed on this front. Roxy aspires to be the greatest pizza witch, despite her parents’ insistence that she focus on more gourmet foods. But her apprenticeship to the Pizza Warlock doesn’t look like it will get her there. It’s a fast, fun tale that I’d recommend to middle-grade readers.

This graphic novel is short-listed for the 2026 Hugo Awards. “The Space Cat” depicts the Okorafor family cat as a space traveler, the son of a cat from Neptune. Periwinkle has a good life, and moving to Nigeria for a year will be just another adventure. But Nigerians don’t like cats, and then there is the alien invasion. It’s a fun and light story, best targeted at a middle-grade reader. If this hadn’t been nominated, however, I would not have read it—it’s not my thing.

This graphic novel is short-listed for the 2026 Hugo Awards. “The Power Fantasy” examines a world where there are only a handful of super-powered individuals, but given their incredible abilities, tackles them as if they are on par with the existing superpowers, like the U.S. and USSR. A delicate balance exists, and it may not be possible to keep them from destroying one another and the rest of humanity with them. This is the best of the nominees I’ve read so far, and will likely be my top choice for the Hugo. I plan to read the next two volumes when I get a chance.

This graphic story is short-listed for the 2026 Hugo Awards. “The Invisible Parade” is a children’s book, depicting Cala as she struggles with the passing of her grandfather on Día de los Muertos. There is a clever and novel play on an old mythology mixed in which I appreciated. And, the art is quite good. I’d recommend this to elementary school aged readers, though it isn’t top-tier as a picture book.
