15 June Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“Shroud” is my fifth book by Adrian Tchaikovsky. In this new release, a humanity ruled by “concerns” encounters life on a moon that never gets light from its star. The narrator is the assistant to the director of the mission, which means she does all the glue work that keeps the mission going. This is a first encounter story that will appeal to hard sci-fi fans. I think it is my second favorite of Tchaikovsky’s works (“Alien Clay” edges this out).

“The Tusks of Extinction” is my fourth novella among the Hugo finalists, and the only one that I’d been planning to read already before the finalists were announced. It takes place in a future after humanity has figured out how to revive extinct species, but the revived mammoths are struggling to survive in the Siberian tundra. The story pulls in two sci-fi concepts, combining then in an unexpected way. I quite liked it and would definitely recommend the book to others. It’s a close call, but I think I have to place it second among the novellas so far.

“The Tainted Cup” was the last of the Hugo novel finalists for me to read. I’d heard good things and planned to read it anyway. It feels like a Holmes–Watson style mystery, from the perspective of the assistant. It is set in a fantasy world, where many people have taken on supernatural body augmentations in service of the empire. There are also kaijū-like leviathans that the empire has built walls to keep out. Our narrator has perfect memory, and he isn’t sure what augmentation his boss, the investigator, has. This book is lots of fun, and I’ve already put in a hold for the second book, which came out a few months ago.

-- Nonfiction --

“Runes” is an approachable scholarly overview of what we know about runes. It covers the three major categories of runes and how their uses changed over time. It also digs into the mythologies around runes that have developed in the past 200 years, which includes Tolkien but also the Nazi fascination which tainted our perception. Interestingly, the new-age notions of fortune telling with runes derives from the fellow the Nazis learned from. I’ve toyed with the idea of learning Old English, so maybe I’ll come back to all this at some point.

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“The Deep Dark” is a Hugo finalist for graphic story and my second read in the category (I read “The Hunger and the Dusk” in single-issue format as it came out). I’ve been a fan of Molly Knox Ostertag for a long time (2012 or 2013), and have read several of her works. This one reads fast, but moves slow as you know something unnatural is happening, but aren’t sure what. The focus is really on the relationships. This book is excellent, and I see I’m going to have a tough time voting in this category.
