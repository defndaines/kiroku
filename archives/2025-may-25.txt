25 May Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“Axiom’s End” takes place in an alternate 2007. The main character’s estranged father is world famous for his leaking of a memo that the U.S. has had first contact with an alien species. The novel has a YA feel at the beginning, though Cora is in her early twenties, but maybe that’s because the set-up is a little gonzo. It is a propulsive read, though I would not consider it particularly deep for a first-contact novel. This is the first book in a trilogy, but I won’t be reading on.

“What Moves the Dead” is my fourth book by T. Kingfisher. I went into the story blind, so didn’t catch on that it is a retelling of Poe’s “The Fall of the House of Usher” until a bit into the book. I then read a summary of the original work to have that grounding. It is a fun twist to the original story. This isn’t Kingfisher’s best, but I still enjoyed. I plan to read the sequel soon, since it is a 2025 Hugo finalist for novella.

I read “Navigational Entanglements” because it is a 2025 Hugo finalist for novella. This is my first time reading de Bodard, though she’s been on my radar for a while. It’s an enjoyable space opera with four women from different clans thrown in with each other to solve a problem. While reading, I did find my spacial awareness lacking, not clearly visualizing the places and transitions. I also found Hạc Cúc’s repetitive self degradation a bit much. I’ll likely read de Bodard again, but I will keep these flaws in mind, as she is a well established author.

“When the Tiger Came Down the Mountain” is a follow-up to “The Empress of Salt and Fortune”, which I read last year. The fifth book in this series of novellas is a 2025 Hugo finalist, so I wanted to read the series. I can say at this point that the books mostly stand-alone, with the same main character. I like the world building, a vaguely Chinese light fantasy. Given how short they are, I should be able to read the next three soon.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

The “Monstress” series has won a number of awards, and volume 9 is a 2025 Hugo finalist for graphic story. The art is gorgeous, capturing the fantasy and steampunk aesthetic well. Volume 1 introduces the characters and is heavy on the world building. We also have a protagonist that very quickly shows incredible power despite her age (O.P., as my son would say). I’m still a little ambivalent about it. I will continue reading the series, assuming that it will get better based solely on the fact that it keeps getting nominated for and winning awards.
