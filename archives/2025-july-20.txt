20 July Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

I’d heard of “Beautyland” at some point last year, but committed to reading it because it was a book club selection. It reads like an autobiography of an alien born in 1977 to an Italian-American mother, who ends up raising her alone. The early parts dragging on a bit, mostly because of how much of the book is covering the narrator’s pre-adult life, when what I signed up for was literary sci-fi. In the end, I quite enjoyed the book. The writing is beautiful and introspective.

“Counterattacks at Thirty” is Sohn Won-Pyung’s second, award-winning book, though I haven’t read “Almond” yet. After some brief set-up to establish the narrator’s birth in 1988, we jump to her life as a 29-year-old, single woman working a dead-end office job. There are some good jabs at Korean culture and capitalism in general, especially after our protagonist meets a man looking to prank the powerful. I liked the work, though I wouldn’t recommend it to folks who aren’t already exposed to modern Korea society.

-- Nonfiction --

“Native Nations” won the Pulitzer for History this year, and that’s a very much deserved award. The writing is quite approachable, not feeling stiff like many academic works, while at the same time this should be mandatory reading as a high school textbook. It covers around 1000 years of history, starting with the rise and downfall of North America cities. We get a realistic assessment of the different nations and their motivations, as opposed to the map dreams of white Europeans planning to take over the continent. This book will jog loose parts of standard U.S. miseducation you didn’t even realize you still accepted.

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --
