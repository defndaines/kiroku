12 October Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

I learned of “The Man Who Saw Seconds” when it won the Locus Award this year. This story grabbed my attention because it expands on a thought experiment I’d already had: what if you could see the future, but only five seconds ahead? The writing it propulsive, like reading an action movie. But then layered on this is an analysis and critique of U.S. society, particularly the way it wields violence. Just one example dropped in the book is that the NYPD would rank in the top 20 of largest militaries in the world. It’s this part of the story that explains why it won the Mark Twain grand prize for satire. Definitely one of the best sci-fi of the year.

-- Nonfiction --

I started following Karakatsanis’ work last year and saw him speak earlier this year. “Copaganda” is a master-class in media analysis. I can barely scratch the surface of nuggets here. Between 2006 and 2024, at least 52,529 guns once owned by police were later used to commit a crime. One-third of all murders by strangers in the U.S. are by police. We see the complicit nature of the media in funneling money into the punishment bureaucracy, increasing the authoritarian base every step of the way. The mainstream media seems to never honestly look at the real reform proposals activists have, instead channeling efforts into more funding for police to keep doing what has already been show to not work for human needs. A must read.

“Red Scare” covers the 1940s and early 1950s, tracking how the U.S. took on a moral panic against “communism” that allowed for purges across the nation. McCarthy was not there at the beginning, but this inveterate scammer made his political career off it; his first attacks against homosexuals, overturning a period where many were open about their sexuality. We also see the destruction of unions. I remember still having a “loyalty oath” to work as a T.A. at UCLA in the ’90s. And there’s the effects on Hollywood. The Red Scare only came to an end because more powerful right-wing politicians had finally had enough. But many of the perpetrators carried on, including McCarthy’s right-hand man, Roy Cohn, who later mentored Trump. Which helps make it clear that it won’t be politicians who get us out of our current period of terror. The book isn’t perfect, but still highly recommended.

“The Korean War” is an excellent survey of the causes and effects of that war. We see the throttled attempts by Koreans to form their own government, totally unacceptable to the U.S. and the Japanese collaborators who they kept in control of the south. The death toll is probably double that of Spain, a country of comparable size that had a civil war just a few years earlier. The book details the slaughter in Jeju by Koreans and the brutality of Noguen-ri, where U.S. soldiers killed women and children on par with My Lai later in Viet Nam, just a month into the war. We also see the efforts at truth and reconciliation made in the South after winning representative government in the ’90s. An inadvertent but perfect companion piece to “Red Scare”, covering the same time period.

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

I plowed through all eight story arcs of “Resident Alien” this week. They are a quick and engaging read, and now I’m interested in checking out the SyFy series. We follow the adventures of an alien, crash landed on Earth and needing to keep hidden until rescue. He takes on the alias of Harry Vanderspeigle, pulled into working as a doctor for a small town in Washington state. While building personal relationships, he also gets involved in solving mysteries that arrive in town. My only hesitancy in recommending the comic is that the portrayal of Natives plays into some seriously cringe mysticism stereotypes. These bits are regular enough that you don’t forget about it, but I hope the T.V. series changed that up.
