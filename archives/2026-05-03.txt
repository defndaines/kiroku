## Fiction

I like Nnedi Okorafor, but for whatever reason I didn’t track this one when it came out. I picked it up now because it is a 2026 Hugo Award Finalist. “Death of the Author” mixes three kinds of chapters: interviews of people in Zelu’s life at some indeterminate time; the narrative of Zelu as her career collapses, prompting her to write a sci-fi book; and the narrative of Ankara, a robot in a future after the last of humanity has died. Zelu is the outlier in her Nigerian-American family, for her artistic career, her sense of adventure, and her inability to walk. The book reads more literary than sci-fi and was definitely a different vibe from other Okorafor books I have read. I liked the book, though it took a while to grow on me. I think it is my second favorite of the Hugo finalists this year.

I won this from Goodreads, so I read a pre-release “uncorrected” proof. I loved the Walter Mosley I read over twenty years ago, but then lost track and have only recently started to get back into his books. “Ghalen” starts at the beginning of Jamilah and Robert’s relationship in 1999, her in med school at UCLA and he a neurodivergent, vegan cook. We follow through to the birth of Ghalen and track his coming of age. The writing is smooth, and I found myself easily pulled into the story. The characters are distinct and mostly believable—I did struggle to understand a pivotal decision that Ghalen makes. I still enjoyed the read. As to the “uncorrected” nature of my copy, there were only like two typos, but there was one glaring oddity that I hope is fixed before publication because it jarred every time it came up: Jamilah is maybe 23 in 1999, but was supposed to have been 3 when her father went MIA in Viet Nam, which means she would have to be at least 29. The father is a trope-y character as well, who was minor enough that he didn’t pull down the overall experience of the book.

## Nonfiction

I’ve read Vincent Bevins’ “If We Burn” twice, so was eager to check out his earlier book. “The Jakarta Method” centers around the events in Indonesia in 1965, but covers the expanse of U.S. foreign policy following WWII through today. It is still unclear how intimately the CIA was involved in the coup and mass murder of perhaps a million leftists, as both countries have not opened all the records yet (if ever). We see the build up to this policy approach, with the destruction of democratic movements in Guatemala, Iraq, Iran, and Brazil. And then we see the glee and hubris as the U.S. and anti-democratic allies push the Jakarta Method elsewhere, sowing chaos to destabilize so that reactionary forces can take control. It’s chilling to realize that the powerful are willing to apply many of the same techniques internally now. Essential reading for all.
