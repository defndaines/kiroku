## Fiction

“On the Calculation of Volume II” picks up right after the conclusion of the previous book. As the initial hope of stepping off the merry-go-round of a year fades, Tara has to think about next steps. She decides to focus on seasons, recapturing the feeling of the year progressing by moving around Europe’s various November 18s to simulate the chilling into winter. An excellent addition to the series, continuing the thoughtful exploration of what being trapped in a day might do to a person over the long run. Definitely continuing the series.

“There Is No Antimemetics Division” started getting a lot of buzz recently, so I had to seek it out. This book feels a bit like a mashup of Nolan’s “Memento”, Cthulhu mythos, and “Something Is Killing the Children”. You learn quickly that there are some ideas which are dangerous, but also nearly impossible to see. The U.K. has an Antimemetics Division, and our protagonist is the director, playing the long game. This book is well written and constructed. Even when you know there are twists and turns, you don’t have much grounding to predict what’s going to happen. Recommended for readers who like concept exploration.

I picked up “Slow Gods” because a number of reviewers who I like mentioned it. This isn’t my first Claire North book, but I didn’t remember that going in. Mawukana was born on a planet in the Shine, where wage slavery is so entrenched that there are four pronouns, two for those who work off their debt and two for those at the top. (It really gives off future capitalist/U.S. vibes.) And then the Slow show up and inform everyone that a binary star will collapse in the future and all planets within 81 light years will eventually be destroyed as a result. What does it mean to know your exact date of death? How do interstellar civilizations deal with a refugee crisis? And why is there a society with only two genders where people obsess about strangers’ genitalia? This is top-notch sci-fi that I’d recommend to everyone. I could see myself re-reading this in the future.

“So ... the important thing is your genitals?” Gebre blurted, when I explained this. “As in ... even if you can’t see someone’s genitals, they are the first thing that is on your mind when you meet someone? It is their defining characteristic, above ethics, work, aptitudes, hobbies, hopes, loves, et cetera?”
“That is one way of looking at it, yes.”

## Nonfiction

Although “Open Veins of Latin America” was written in 1970, the content is still extremely relevant today, especially as the U.S. has resumed open aggression against our neighbors to the south. The statistics, obviously, won’t be up to date, and the hope for Allende is a bit chilling. But this should be considered essential reading—a primer—for anyone who wants to understand why Latin America is the way it is today, from the role played by Spain and Portugal through to the primary role of the U.S. in keeping these countries’ economies and peoples subservient to capitalism’s need for resources. The edition I read has an epilogue written seven or eight years after the original edition. Oh, and if you see mention online that the author recanted his opinions here, that’s an intentionally gross misinterpretation of a statement he made a few years ago.
