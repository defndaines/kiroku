## Short Fiction

“Laser Eyes Ain’t Everything” is a 2026 Hugo finalist for Short Story. RazorBeam developed laser eyes after being hit by alien spaceship debris. Then she was laid off from the City Planner’s office. So, she decided to join the union of the Super-Abled to effect change. But, their building is not ADA compliant, and the union is unwilling to install a ramp to make it so she can get her wheelchair into the building. Undeterred, she still seeks to use her new eyes to help society. This is a great short story, even though I saw the ending coming. Definitely recommend you track it down.

I have a copy of Kim Cho-yeop’s “지구 끝의 온실”, but I cannot read it. So, I was delighted to see that her short-story collection “If We Cannot Go at the Speed of Light” had been translated. The stories range from near to distant future sci-fi, and each takes a novel concept and builds upon it. And, as great sci-fi does, it holds up a mirror to our own society, whether it be class distinctions or gender discrimination. I definitely recommend the collection and look forward to more of her work getting translated.

“The River Has Roots” is a 2026 Hugo finalist for Novella. This fantasy tale verges on poetry at times. The Hawthorn sisters come from a family with a deep history of tending magical willows. But as they come of age, what will love and courting bring? There is the lure of the fae, whose world they brush up against, and there is an insistent local eager to take the eldest sister in marriage. This work is more literary than your average fantasy. I enjoyed it, though I don’t this it will be my first choice for Novella.

“In My Country” is a 2026 Hugo finalist for Short Story. The narrator lives in a dystopian world, where a neighbor who lives in a blue house has the right to come and go as they please, and the First Citizen encourages everyone to to “question” them. But the father has raised two kids alone, his wife having left for the “far fields” after the birth of their second child. And his son has starting making a name for himself writing fiction that is ambiguous. This is top-notch allegory within allegory that I highly recommend. It might be my favorite of an excellent group of short story finalists.

“A Long and Speaking Silence” is book seven in The Singing Hills Cycle. I plowed through five of the books last year alone, so grabbed this as soon as it was published. Chih is now an ordained cleric, as they arrive in Luntien. Working at a restaurant to make ends meet until their stipend arrives, they anticipate the coming festival. But there is also a flood of refugees in town, raising tensions between the displaced and the locals. I really liked this addition to the series and would probably rank it was one of the better ones.

I read at least part of “Up the Line” as a teenager, though during this (re)reading the parts I remembered clearly were quite early in the book. Jud starts work as a time courier after a fast friendship with Sam. In the 2050s, time travel is not only possible, it is commercialized. Besides acting as a tour guide, a courier must be able to think in four dimensions and avoid the paradoxes one risks by traveling to the past. I haven’t read this many descriptions of female bodies and sex in all the books I’ve read in the last five years (though I’ve read more graphic descriptions in romances). While the ins and outs of time travel paradoxes are core to the plot, so much else is about sex, including the question of playing out your Oedipal complex across the centuries. And trigger warning: besides a few descriptions of sexual assault, a pivotal antagonist is a pedophile. The writing is propulsive and the sci-fi interesting, but I don’t think I’d recommend this to anyone.

## Nonfiction

When “Things in Nature Merely Grow” came out last year, I heard the buzz, but made the decision that I wouldn’t read such a difficult book unless it won an award. This past week it won the Pulitzer. Yiyun Li is a professor at Princeton who has lost both of her sons to suicide. In the eloquent prose of a literary scholar, she guides us through that experience and its aftermath. We see what it takes to make it through the unthinkable, a parent outliving not one but two children. This is not an easy read, but it also isn’t a tearjerker. You’ll be reading and then suddenly get struck by the profundity of something she has said and have to pause. Not for the faint of heart, but highly recommended.

## Graphic Novels

After a marathon catch-up in 2024, I finally got volume 12 of “Saga”. The family has taken refuge in a circus, having managed to get off everyone’s radar for long enough to finally catch their breath. This means new friends, but the young prince is still dealing with his issues. And it doesn’t mean they aren’t still being tracked. Although not the best installment, I’m committed to continuing the series.
