22 June Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“Negative Scholarship on the Fifth State of Being” won the Nebula for Best Novelette. A doctor is presented with an alien patient for whom there is no diagnostic criteria. There’s a nice critique of medicine and medical systems in here, though that isn’t the focus of the story. I quite liked it, and among the handful of sci-fi novelettes I’ve read from 2024, I can see why this won the award. It’s available for free in issue 218 of Clarkesworld.

I came across “The Lady of the Yellow-Painted Library” while browsing the Ignyte Award winners. This Nigerian horror-fantasy short story is read by LeVar Burton. It’s a fun little tale about the consequences of not returning your library books. Definitely recommend checking this out via the LeVar Burton Reads podcast.

I’m not sure how I stumbled across “The Secret”. It’s available through khōréō, a speculative fiction magazine and podcast I also just learned about. This is a fun sci-fi short story touching upon the rights to our likenesses. Takano has won several awards in Japan, so I might try to track down more of her writing at some point.

“Calypso” is my final poem of the Hugo finalists. Unlike the other finalists that fit on a few pages, this is a 215-page poem. There’re some interesting ideas in here, and the poetry format is nice. But I also lost attention frequently and didn’t care about the outcome by the end. I haven’t figured out the significance of the two main male characters being named after dragon slayers. Maybe my least favorite of the Hugo poems this year.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“We Called Them Giants” is my third Hugo finalist for Graphic Story, and my second time reading Gillen. We are immediately in a post-apocalyptic situation where the narrator has no idea what happened. The survivors quickly form up in stereotypical, capitalist-culture fashion, and the search for food and safety is on. But then we have aliens, who are much more powerful than humans with indiscernible motives. This story really does nothing for me.
