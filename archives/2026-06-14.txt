## Fiction

It’s hard to say much about sequels to anyone who hasn’t read the preceding volumes already. “On the Calculation of Volume III” continues Tara’s loop of 18 November, building upon the major reveal at the end of “Volume II”. I still quite liked this book, though I think that the first two books in the series had a bigger impact on me. I am also still eager to continue on with the series and hope to read book four in the next month or so.

When I saw that a major epic fantasy had been translated from Korean, I definitely had to check it out. “The Heart of the Nhaga” is an adventurous tale of intrigue, set in a world clearly inspired by Korean folklore, though I don’t know enough about the lore to know how much is wholly from the mind of the author. The four races are brought together: a human, a tokkebi, and a rekon are tasked with protecting a nhaga and bringing him to a temple. We shift from different perspectives, and see the intrigue that looks to thwart the plan, both intentionally and unintentionally. There is fun world-building, with the lure of more intriguing adventures to come. This is the first of four books, so I’ll have to wait for the rest to be translated.

As a book-club book, I came into “Transcription” without a sense of what it was about. Framed in three parts, each identified by a hotel that provides part of the setting, we see the worlds of the narrator, his 90-year-old mentor, and that mentor’s son. It is set during COVID, and the narrator must separate from his family to interview the elderly professor. And then he drops his phone—the intended recording device—into water before the interview. The book touches on a number of topics, from our relationships to technology and to fathers and children. This is all about the characters and their coming to terms with the world. I liked the book overall, though I struggle to think who I would recommend it to. I’m open to reading more of the author’s work.

## Short Fiction

I picked up “We Begin Where Infinity Ends” when it won the Locus Award for novelette. In a near-future Nigeria, three teenagers with an interest in technology build their friendship as they work on a project to improve the streetlights in their neighborhood. Although their hacking on technology is what frames their connection, this is really a coming-of-age tale. We get inside Naeto’s head as he faces the emotions the changing circumstances bring out in him. Although I liked the story, I have not read enough of the Locus finalists to see why this one was chosen.

I came across “The Name Ziya” because it was a finalist for Nebula Award for novelette this year. Set in a mildly steampunk fantasy world, Ziya is part of the first cohort of her people to attend a prestigious college in the imperial center. To afford the tuition, she must sell three of the five characters of her name. We follow her through the three years of school, and see the effect that sacrificing parts of your name have on her and her fellow students from the periphery. The analogy here is a bit on-the-nose, but it’s still and interesting and well-told story.

“Human Voices” is a novelette finalist for the Ignyte Award this year. Kos is a siren who wakes up in a bathtub, held captive by Irina and her younger sister, Livvy. We then come to understand how and why Irina took a siren captive. But what do they plan to do with the Kos, who seemingly unbeknownst to them, is dying from the captivity? I liked the writing style and the way the mystery unfolds, and I wouldn’t be disappointed if this won the award.

## Nonfiction

I’d been tempted by “Positive Obsession” when I saw it in the bookstore, and it is a 2026 finalist for a Related Work Hugo Award. This biography of Octavia E. Butler covers her personal history as well as the historical circumstances in which she composed each of her books. This is a must-read for Butler fans, but be warned that there are spoilers of most of the books, so I would only recommend it if you’ve already worked through much of her writing already.

## Graphic Novels

The concept behind “The Seasons” caught my attention. There are four season sisters, orphaned ten years ago when their detective parents disappeared. Spring is the youngest and tasked with saving her family when Autumn sends a letter warning of impending doom. I liked the art and style of the story, though in the end I wasn’t completely pulled in, probably because two of the sisters aren’t particularly likable. I’m curious enough to see what happens, but we’ll see if that is sustained when the next volume becomes available to me.

I’d forgotten I had a pull on the TPBs of “Wonder Woman” until it came in. We start with a flashback to Wonder Woman taking down Mouse Man after he used giant mice to take over the capital. Then we jump to the current timeline, when Diana breaks a covenant to visit the island now ruled by Mouse Man, all in order to track down the fugitive Amazon Emilie. And at the end of that storyline, we get a two-issue tale of returning to Paradise Isle. There was context missing from the story that presumably derives from other books I haven’t read. But overall this was a less interesting turn in the storyline, and I am not planning to continue the series from here.

I had no idea what “Ocultos” was about going in, but figured it was worth checking out. There are many short tales that are sometimes connected, but often not. We do get some return to earlier parts, but mostly the parts stand alone in a way. I did like the art, but I often felt like the artist was trying to convey an emotional state that I wasn’t able to key into. I didn’t hate this, but I also couldn’t see recommending it to anyone.
