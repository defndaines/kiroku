## Fiction

“The Everlasting” was my last read of the 2026 Hugo Award finalists for novel. Owen dislikes his dad’s antiwar efforts and signs onto military service. Discharged with a medal, he becomes a scholar of the founding myths of Dominion, in particular of Sir Una Everlasting. Then the new chancellor provides a unique opportunity to “translate” a newly discovered text of Una’s adventures. With this framed as a romance, I wasn’t sure what to expect, but this book plays out rather well. It’s quite frustrating for a good chunk, with a pro-war protagonist who is rather unlikable. Definitely recommended, and this might be my favorite of the Hugo novels this year.

## Short Fiction

I went blind into “Cinder House”, a novella finalist for the 2026 Hugo Awards. We start with the murder of 16-year-old Ella and her father, leading to her haunting the house. Only her stepmother and stepsisters can see her, and because of her connection to the house, she cannot resist the compulsion to do their bidding in maintaining the house. Will a fairy provide her a means of escaping the situation? This is definitely the most innovative retelling of “Cinderella” I’ve ever encountered. There are clever additions and twists that made it even more compelling. Definitely recommended, and currently my favorite of the Hugo novellas this year.

I read the novella “Murder by Memory” because it is a finalist for the 2026 Hugo Awards. Dorothy wakes up in a body that isn’t hers. Given her role as investigator on an interstellar ship, this can only mean that there is a crime to uncover. In this case, not only has someone been murdered, but memory backups have been destroyed, including Dorothy’s. This is a nice who-done-it that builds upon an alternative concept to the generation ship: human memories are stored and replaced into their recreated bodies in order to keep alive the crew for the hundreds of years it will take to reach their destination. Although not bad, currently my least favorite of the novella nominees this year.

## Nonfiction

This is my first Joan Didion, picked up for book club. “Slouching Towards Bethlehem” is a collection of Didion’s early journalism and essays. It includes profiles of figures across the political spectrum, meditations on places personal or famous, and pieces with a philosophical bent. The first essay, on the double standards applied to a woman convicted of murdering her husband, was the highlight. Then things rapidly go downhill with a fawning portrait of the racist John Wayne. She highlights a couple more horrible people, like Hughes and Hutchins. And later we get a takedown of a Hollywood myth, where she explores her contempt for “Dr. Strangelove“ while praising the HUAC snitch Kazan. Her writing is beautiful, an exemplification of the “New Journalism” trend she helped pioneer. I could see giving her work another chance, but I wouldn’t recommend this one.

## Graphic Novels

I had tried to follow “Helen of Wyndhorn” when it first came out, but my pull got messed up. In the 1930s, Helen’s father passes away, leaving her in the care of a governess and her estranged grandfather. Helen’s father was a pulp writer, crafting Conan-like fantasy tales. Working through the trauma, Helen realizes that something is not quite normal at her grandfather’s mansion. Maybe her father’s stories weren’t fiction after all? This is a wordy and slow-moving tale by the team that did “Supergirl: Woman of Tomorrow”. I liked it and would recommend it if you liked the Supergirl book or are a Conan fan with a historical or literary bent.

I’d heard of “Tank Girl”, maybe in the early ’90s before the movie came out; I don’t know that I ever saw the movie. This book collects the earliest strips, colorized, along with some photographs of the artists and friends. Set 200 years into the future, Tank Girl roams Australia, shagging kangaroos and defending herself against bounty hunters. There are fun and silly elements here, and even some good political jabs, but the work is also dated. There are references to political and celebrity figures of the late ’80s (in Britain, where it was written and published) that don’t land any more. Then there is the racist portrayal of a voodoo plastic “surgeon”. I can see the appeal of the character, but I cannot see myself reading any more.
