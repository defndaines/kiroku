## Short Fiction

Despite its fame, I only just got around to reading “The Lottery”. This short story depicts a small town on the annual lottery day, as everyone gathers in the town square to see who is selected this year. There are rumors that other towns have done away with the lottery, but our town is proud to hold to tradition, even if they don’t know clearly where it came from. Having read other versions and interpretations of this story, it’s still worth reading the classic. It’s a 20-minute read, available online from The New Yorker, where it was originally published.

## Nonfiction

“Everyone Who Is Gone Is Here” is a journalistic history of post-WWII immigration to the United States, with an emphasis on the “Northern Triangle” of El Salvador, Guatemala, and Honduras. Blitzer follows several different individuals over time to highlight that history, as U.S. intervention into the economies of Central America helps crush any semblance of progressive social improvements. For anyone who only recently started paying attention to immigration issues, this is good background. An important takeaway from the book is how the current administration’s approach is not really that different from previous administrations. This book is a beast, and the last fifth started to feel like a slog to me, but the information is solid.

## Graphic Novels

The main story of the latest issue of “The Savage Sword of Conan” has Conan and his companions trapped in a paradise that cannot possibly be what it seems. A good issue overall.

It’s been a decade since I last read “A Wizard of Earthsea”, so I was looking forward to this graphic novel rendition spurring me to continue the series later this year. The artist got approval from Le Guin’s son, and the text is almost entirely pulled directly from the original. We follow Sparrowhawk from his early days, discovering magic from an aunt, to his overabundance of pride while studying at Roke. The art is top-notch, and I would recommend this to anyone, whether you’ve read the original or not.
