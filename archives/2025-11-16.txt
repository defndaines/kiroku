16 November Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“Mongrels” is my third book by Stephen Graham Jones (not counting comic books). I picked it up because I’m always looking for a good werewolf story. Little did I know that this is not a horror book, but instead a slice-of-life, coming-of-age tale. We hop back and forth across time and locations around the southern U.S., following our protagonist as he grows up raised by his aunt and uncle after his mother died in childbirth. Life as a poor family is already hard, and they are frequently uprooted when their werewolf identities are at risk of being exposed. This is definitely my favorite book of his. The perspective shifts are nice, and the narrator’s voice reflects a worldview that leaves us wondering.

“When Sleeping Women Wake” follows a mother, daughter, and their maid after the Japanese invasion of Shanghai settles them in Hong Kong, only to be invaded and occupied two years later, in 1941. Each of them must make it through the occupation any way they can. Their desire to resist leads them to the East River Column, which fought against the Japanese until the surrender in 1945. The writing is good, keeping the reader engaged with a mix of action, intrigue, and romance. The coincidences that bring characters back into contact at key points are a little eye-rolling. I also wondered at the verisimilitude at times. Some of this is rooted in Biyu’s loyalty, but the biggest is that the word “Communist” only appears once in the entire book, even though the real ERC was led by the CCP. Still recommended.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“I May Be a Guild Receptionist, but I’ll Solo Any Boss to Clock Out on Time” is such a silly premise that I wanted to give it a try. Alina has a job as a receptionist, one of the most stable jobs there is. She is in a good place in her life, mostly ... until the overtime kicks in and ruins her routine. I was actually surprised at how quickly the title plays out and her identity is uncovered, but it doesn’t detract from the pacing and humor of the story. And, refreshingly, the story is not sexualized, the one thing I worried about based upon Alina’s disproportionate features. I liked the story and might continue on with volume two, though not right away.

“The Savage Sword of Conan Reforged” is a reprinting with colorization of works originally published in the 1970s. “The Frost Giant’s Daughter” (Roy Thomas and Barry Windsor-Smith, 1971) is a cornerstone story. As the last man standing on a battlefield in the north, he is lured away by a basically naked woman into the snowy hills. This one doesn’t age quite as well, with the depiction of Conan’s intentions, but also feels like the battle could have been drawn out a little more. “Tower of the Elephant” (Roy Thomas, John Buscema, and Alfred Alcala, 1977) is another of the cornerstone Conan stories, which each reboot tackles at some point. Here we see Conan on one of his first adventures after leaving Cimmeria, joining forces with another to burgle the famous heart gem thought to be worth a fortune. Overall, a great collection of both classic stories and iconic artists who have portrayed Conan.
