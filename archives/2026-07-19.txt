## Fiction

“Honey in the Wound” is a multi-generational story of 20th century Korea and the legacy of Japanese brutality. Each generation has some supernatural talent, but the core of the story follows Young-Ja, whose emotions get infused into her cooking. At first, this means her customers enjoy the happiness she felt when she made her yakgwa. After tragedy, this means something else. This is not a happy history, and the author pulls few punches in describing the horrors. I was a bit skeptical of the magical realism element, but the way it ties in does not detract from the historical importance. Highly recommended.

“Kim Jiyoung, Born 1982” comes across as a biography of a Korean woman who would have been 34 at the time of release (2016). We follow her from childhood, through education, employment, and ultimately to marriage and a child. Along the way, we see the position of women in Korea, accompanied by statistics that show the country ranks dead last among “advanced” nations and the feeble progress that has been made by women since the ’80s. It does read a little dry at times, but by the end you realize that this is on purpose. The gut punch at the end is chef’s kiss. A quick read that I definitely recommend.

## Short Fiction

“What Stalks the Deep” is a finalist for the 2026 Hugo Awards, and the third novella in the “Sworn Soldier” series. Alex travels to America (circa 1898) to help Dr. Denton, whose cousin Oscar has gone missing while exploring a West Virginian coal mine. Oscar has written of a strange red light and a pearlescent cave. Then an uncharacteristic telegram warns Denton to stay away, after which Oscar no longer responded. This is a good addition to the series, and I think I liked this story more than the previous book, “What Feasts at Night”. Recommended if you liked the first novella.

“The Summer War” is the last 2026 Hugo Awards novella finalist for me to read, and my first Naomi Novik. Prosper borders the “summer lands”, and the two kingdoms have been at war for a century. But Celia’s father seems to have brought the war to an end. When her oldest brother, Argent, returns only to part for good, Celia’s sorcery and menses manifest for the first time, inadvertently cursing her beloved brother. Alongside her other brother, she prepares to enter the summer lands to uncurse her brother. This reads as a fairy tale and is well composed, though a bit contrived at times. Of the novella finalists this year, I found it one of the weaker choices. I didn’t dislike it, and I still plan to read Novik’s more famous works.

“Joanna’s Bodies” was a 2024 Nebula finalist for novelette. I picked it up because I thought Triantafyllou’s “Loneliness Universe” was one of the best I read last year. Eleni has to perform an occult ritual to keep her friend Joanna around after her unfortunate death. But Joanna can only possess a body for so long before she has to give it back to its owner. But the stress of finding new bodies isn’t the only strain on the two friends’ relationship. This story is an homage to the film “Jennifer’s Body”, and I don’t recommend reading it until you’ve re-watched the film. (You have watched it, right?!?) I liked the tale, and I’ll be tracking more of this author’s work down.

“Ice, Rock, Empathy” finds us on Europa, Jupiter’s icy moon, with first contact between humanity and the native population. A human who crashed on the moon interacts with the locals who are trying to save them and their memories. For the Europans, this is their first real comprehension of life beyond their planet. A quick first-contact story seen mostly through the eyes of the locals.

In “The Life and Times of the Extraterrestrial Potoo”, Isaac has never excelled at anything and has been sent to a planet to harvest valuable materials. Then he loses contact with his bosses and must continue to make his way to a remote destination without backup. Along the way he encounters a local who has a humanoid form. He’d been briefed on what to expect from the locals, but Dave is something else. A good short story on expectations and friendships, and a little more.
