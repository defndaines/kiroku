## Short Fiction

As a birthday present to myself, I bought three Claire Keegan hardbacks (two of which I’ve read already). “So Late in the Day” collects three stories. The titular tale follows Cathal as he contemplates a life that could have been. “The Long and Painful Death” has an author retreating to a remote northwest island to focus on her writing. “Antarctica” depicts a women looking for a break from her married life. The first story is brutal and amazing, an easy five stars, and the highlight of the collection. Keegan is a favorite of mine, and I look forward to reading all her works.

“Brawler” collects nine short stories with an amazing variety of tone and characters. Many of the stories rest on a twist, and some are quite dark. Every story is good, and some are excellent. The first story, “Wind”, with its retelling of a grandmother’s story via her mother’s recollection hit the hardest for me. I definitely recommend the collection.

“Six People to Revise You” is a 2026 Hugo finalist for Short Story. The narrator must get six people to fill out a form in order to undergo Revision, and vaguely defined procedure that will make your life better for a cost. I liked the story, though I think it is in last place among an excellent crop of short stories up for the Hugo this year.

“10 Visions of the Future” is a 2026 Hugo finalist for Short Story. I read this a second time because I was a little confused I’d read it but hadn’t tracked it. It’s so quick that you finish it in no time. This is a clear allegory with dealing with the current world as it collapses. A good story, though not my favorite of this year’s nominees.

## Manga

Volumes 3 and 4 of “Akira” move the story beyond the anime. The different factions struggle to take control of Akira, as he has yet to reawaken his power. Volume 4 comes with trigger warnings, as there’s sexual assault and rape used gratuitously to motivate some characters and define others. I’m hoping to finish the last two volumes next week.

## Graphic Novels

I read “Lindsey Cheng Dates a White Boy!!!” after seeing that the author was at a local book launch (I didn’t attend). Chinese-American Lindsey if at college, studying hard and starting a band with two friend. Then she starts dating a white guy who is part of a popular and established band. This book is squarely targeted at a younger reader, as Lindsey must learn a life lesson about shitty men and their egos. And there’s a fun little (side) story at the end. I liked the art style, but wouldn’t recommend this to anyone over 20.

“Amalgam” covers three generations of the author’s family, starting with immigration from Poland. The grandfather lands in New York and enters the garment trade, aligning himself and future generations with the trade and the union. The author is an artist known for her block-print illustrations in U.S. magazines. I liked the overall content of this family history, and it could act as an intro to the 20th century history of garment workers. The art style is interesting, but the text was sometimes hard to read.

I picked up “Doom’s Division” entirely because it was written by Yoon Ha Lee. I don’t follow mainstream superhero comics and so was not particularly familiar with the Dr. Doom taking over the world plot that this fits into. I wasn’t familiar with any of the characters beforehand. The story isn’t horrible, and doesn’t rely on you knowing the characters ahead of time, but it was also pretty lackluster, if not typical. The Korean flavor of the primary heroes is the main selling point.
