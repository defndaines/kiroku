31 August Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“Munmun” is my first DNF (did not finish) of the year. This book was called out by Jordan Ifueko as being particularly funny. I got 10% in, and it just wasn’t doing it for me. The characters are diminutive beings in an alternate Los Angeles, where how much power and money you have affects the size of your body. But our characters also mold dreams, potentially earning them favor. They set off to find a husband for the sister, someone who has “munmun” (money). The allegory is quite on-the-nose, and I understand the humor, it just isn’t my style.

I hadn’t read “Inferno” before, but had a good sense of its content, including understanding that many of the people encountered in Hell are the author’s enemies from Tuscany. It is rather interesting how dated so many of the references are, yet the poem remains a classic. I picked this up now in preparation for “Katabasis”. The conception of sins and punishment seem like ideas still held by some to this day, including that everyone born before Jesus is stuck in Hell. And Virgil’s role as guide in the story reminds me that I want to read the “Aeneid” (which I saw has a new translation).

-- Nonfiction --

I read “Pleasure Activism: The Politics of Feeling Good” for book club. I thought this was going to be something else. The “activism” of the title misled me, and I’d rather call this something like self-care for activists, though that doesn’t quite capture it. If you have prudish ideas about sex or gender or drugs, this book could help push your boundaries. It does a good job of countering the mistaken idea that “movement work” should be based on sacrifice. There are a couple places in the book that feel like advice from a place of privilege. But I agree, “Our misery only serves those who wish to control us.”
