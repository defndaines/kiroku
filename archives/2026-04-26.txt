## Fiction

I was a bit nervous to pick up “Hexes of the Deadwood Forest”, given its poor ratings online. Anna Frenza is the CEO of Poland’s largest oil company. But she loses her job after being filmed having sex with a tree during a bout of somnambulism. Yes, you read that right, and the first section of this book is gonzo and hilarious. The majority of the book then jumps back to the 15 and 1600s, following three generations of women at the receiving end of the witch hunts. I may not have appreciated this as much if I hadn’t recently read “Caliban and the Witch”. When the book returns to Anna at the end, it is more poignant for the large digression, but I could see where the middle section of the book will lose a lot of readers.

I heard about “Woman at Point Zero” from a mention by Peter Linebaugh. Framed by a visit to the prison where Firdaus is kept, scheduled for execution, this story recounts a woman’s life in Egypt. Her parents let her get a secondary education, but then there are no expectations of her beyond marriage. When her parents die, she must live with the uncle who had already sexually assaulted her as a child. We continue with her life, toward an understanding of how and why she is to be executed for killing a man. This is a powerful story, though it can feel a little slow at times. The ending drives everything home masterfully.

## Nonfiction

“Not “A Nation of Immigrants”” is my second (amazing) book by Dunbar-Ortiz. This look at American history and immigration is framed around the “nation of immigrants” myth that came to prominence in the late ’50s thanks to JFK. She systematically breaks down the myth, demonstrating quite clearly that the U.S. is a settler-colonial state. After defining some important terms, like “fiscal military state”, “genocide”, and “doctrine of discovery” (and ripping apart “Hamilton”), each chapter looks at the different groups of people that make up the U.S. today and how they fit into the narrative and reality of our history. This is essential reading for anyone interested in U.S. history and particularly in the questions around immigration.

## Graphic Novels

Picking up “The Last Session” was a no-brainer for me, given its D&D adjacent setting. Friendships and a role-playing party form at an after-school club for LGBT kids and allies. Four years later, life has changed for everyone, and the campaign that started it all is coming to its dramatic conclusion. But the GM has a girlfriend joining the party, adding to the tension around the pending dispersal of the players across the country. In the vein of Ostertag’s “Dungeon Club”, this graphic novel mixes real-world interactions with in-game action to tell a complete story. Definitely recommended.

“Shadows of the Sea” is set in a world where animals play the same roles in society, like a bomb-sniffing dog or a mule pulling a cart, but they walk on two legs, wear clothes, and speak like everyone else. The dog protagonist has been discharged from his duties and comes across a woman attacked for having bested the town bully at a public competition. As they travel together, they face their individual traumas. This is a touching book, and the world is interesting and beautifully drawn. The primary warning I would give is that the woman, Doris, speaks in a heavy accent, which I had to sound out (in my head at least) to sometimes understand what she was saying; even near the end of the book, I struggled with her dialogue.
