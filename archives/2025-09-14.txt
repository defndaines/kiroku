14 September Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“The Raven Scholar” is the first book in an expected epic fantasy trilogy. Neema has been the High Scholar to the emperor the last eight years, but now it is time to select the next emperor. This involves eight candidates competing in trials to determine who will succeed. But then there is a murder. This is a beautifully describe world, with fascinating characters and an evolving mystery. Early on, I found myself passionately liking or disliking different characters. 650 pages flew by, and I am eagerly awaiting the next book in the series. I may have to check out the author’s other work in the meantime.

I would have DNF’ed “Perfection” if it wasn’t so short and for a book club. While fiction, it reads like a journalistic recap of the lives of a Millennial Italian couple who spend their twenties and early thirties as ex-pats in Berlin. They work as freelance digital creatives, and we explore many aspects of their lives as we review what it must have been like for middle-class people to live through a certain period of Berlin’s history. There is no dialogue in the book. It was shortlisted for the International Booker, and is longlisted for the National Book Award, so my main goal at the book club meeting will be to wrap my head around why people like this book.

“Volatile Memory” came out less than two months ago, so I must have seen a review that got it in my holds. It’s the first part of a planned duology. Wylla works as a scavenger, but her ship and mask are barely keeping her afloat. Then a calls goes out to retrieve a high-tech mask from a nearby planet. This mask is different. This is a fast-paced book with intriguing world building. And, along the way, it digs into deeper topics. Definitely recommend.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“The Flavors of Iraq” is a memoir collecting a thousand tweets by the author, who was a journalist in Iraq as the U.S. destroyed the country. Born in France to Iraqi parents, Feurat would spend his summers in Baghdad and Fallujah with extended family. He grew up seeing the aftermath of the 1990s invasion and witnessing the degradations as a result of U.S. sanctions against the people of Iraq. In college for journalism when the U.S. invaded again in 2003, he dedicates the next several years of his life to reporting on the conditions there. There are excellent graphics, but it’s barely a graphic novel. I recommend it if you can handle the material.

“Cry Wolf Girl” is a short tale with a play on the “boy who cried wolf” story. The art is nice, and the text is a quick read. I wasn’t particularly impressed by the story, even though the twist is cute.

“The Pass” is an emotional memoir of the artist’s experience with the birth of their second child. Their son is born with a heart defect that is quite rare. The family struggles with his condition, but the maternal grandfather is able to help out and grow a bond with his grandson as he struggles with cancer himself. The art is the typical B.D. style.
