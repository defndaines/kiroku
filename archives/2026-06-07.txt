## Fiction

I was already interested in reading “Taiwan Travelogue” when it was longlisted for the International Booker, so it was a no-brainer to pick it up now that it has won. It is set in 1938, from the perspective of a writer from Nagasaki visiting Taiwan. Aoyama plans to spend an entire year in the southern colony, and she is fortunate to get a Taiwanese woman to travel with her as translator. Aoyama is focused on eating the local cuisine wherever they go, and the descriptions of food in this book will have you salivating. But there are so many layers to this story: romance, colonialism, what it means to be a tourist. Definitely recommended.

## Short Fiction

When I learned that “The Luminous Fairies and Mothra” was the basis for the original movie, I had to buy this. The novella has three chapters, each written by a different author. The bulk of the book is actually the translator’s notes with details about the history and context of the story. It was cool to learn that the political leanings were already there intentionally from the get-go. A must for all Mothra fans.

## Nonfiction

I have a friend (who speaks German) who is a fan of Stefan Zweig and recommended “Magellan” to me a long time ago, but I’ve only now gotten around to reading it. This is an approachable biography of Magellan along with a history of the first circumnavigation of the Earth. The writing is even handed, praising Magellan where appropriate, but also honest about his failings. This reads at times like an adventure story, with tension around what will happen. I enjoyed this and would recommend it to anyone who is interested in the subject.

## Manga

I suppose it was a primary character who is a bike mechanic which got me to step outside my usual genres and pick up “Takahashi from the Bike Shop”. This is a romance comic, where 30-year-old Hanno Tomoko feels the pressure to be in a relationship, but isn’t sure about the men in her office. She’s also unsure of herself, and we get her inner turmoil as a doormat. Takahashi is more straightforward and brash, but kind. I didn’t find the characters as grating as they could be if written more clichéd. I was tempted to continue with the series, but for now I’ll leave it for later.

## Graphic Novels

I finished the “The Power Fantasy” trilogy this week. Although not perfect, I thought it was excellent. We get more history behind the superpowers, as their conflicts come to a head in the (1999) present. Definitely recommended.

“Home Sick Pilots” hit my radar from a couple directions, including a BookTube recommendation and that it’s the same artist as “The Power Fantasy”. Set in 1994, three friends have their own punk band, and in a rivalry with another band, explore a haunted house as a possible concert location. But it turns out the house is actually haunted, and it can go mecha! It’s such a quirky story, that escalates in ways I wasn’t expecting. There’s also some social commentary along the way. Recommended.
