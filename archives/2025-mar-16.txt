16 March Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“Shoko’s Smile: Stories”, Choi Eunyoung, is a collection of seven short stories. Several deal with real historical situations that sometimes come up unexpectedly, like the Sewol Ferry disaster. Some of them only sink in after you get dates later in the story, like realizing where certain events fall on the timeline of the Korean War and the April Revolution (4.19) of 1960. All of the stories feel very real and personal. Many of them dig into the experience of being female in South Korea. I hope to see more from this author in the future.

“The Dream Hotel”, Laila Lalami is the most stressful book I’ve read in a long time, if not ever. A Moroccan-American woman is returning home from a work trip to London when the screeners at LAX flag her as an imminent risk. Her score is now high enough that they believe she will soon commit a crime. This story is in the spirit of Kafka and Philip K. Dick, exploring a near future where A.I. is used to predict crime. Highly recommended, and I will be reading her other work when I get a chance.

“Parable of the Talents” is a classic, one of the greats of dystopian sci-fi. While “Sower” takes place in our current time (2024–2027) and eerily anticipated so much, “Talents” is the one with the “make America great again” president that chills you to the bone in how familiar it sounds. The Earthseed excerpts in this book are on point, and I found myself dwelling on each of them. A must-read. Last year, I was unsure if I’d read the book. I think my confusion arose from reading both books before Butler passed away and having it lodged in my head that I hadn’t completed the series, which was intended to be a trilogy. I’m glad to have reread this. I plan to read all of Butler’s books.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“FTL, Y’all!: Tales From the Age of the $200 Warp Drive” is a collection of 25 stories from different artists, all based on the premise that in the near future the schematics for a faster-than-light drive are made available on the Internet. All these stories are independent, with different takes, from the early effects of such a device to the longer term consequences. Such a large collection (almost 300 pages) is bound to have duds alongside some winners, but it was still a fun concept to see explored.
