Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

I tracked down the short story “The Pedestrian” after it was mentioned in the book “Life After Cars”. In 2053, Leonard goes for a walk in his neighborhood. It’s a quiet, meditative thing, with no activity around except for the faint sounds coming from houses engaged with their nightly entertainment. Published in 1951, the story foretells the effects of owning a TV, a device that had only taken off in the last couple years. This is a fun, quick tale, freely available online.

I picked up the “Eberron: Forge of the Artificer” rulebook last year and slowly read through it cover to cover. It’s a supplement to the existing “Eberron: Rising from the Last War” book from 2019. The classes, species, and feats are updated for the 2024 D&D edition, but many parts of the book also reference back to the earlier book. The last half of the book is a resource for DMs and how to build different styles of campaigns in the world. Other than the artificer class updates, this is a book only for folks engaging in an Eberron campaign.

-- Nonfiction --

For me, “Life After Cars” is preaching to the choir, but it is still an excellent overview of all the harms that the automobile has caused and the ways we could improve society if there were fewer of them. This primarily applies to city life, where greater than 20% of the space is dedicated to personal car storage. The environmental effects are numerous, beyond the exhaust we are all aware of, extending to noise and tire pollution. All this in a society where a third of people cannot drive. The book also addresses the “bikelash” that is pretty much inevitable every time a community decides to share public space with pedestrians and cyclists. This is a must-read.

“When We Walk By” is a thorough survey of homelessness in America. It looks at the many myths and misconceptions around homelessness. It explores the systems we have in place that cause and respond to the problem. And it wraps up with some proposals for solutions, to fix the systems as well as “our humanity”. The authors are involved with Miracle Messages, and there is an emphasis on “relational poverty” which the organization seeks to address. I found the exploration of paternalism to be on point. I would recommend this as an introductory text for anyone seeking to overcome prejudices and wrong ideas about homelessness. For anyone who has more extensive experience or reading on the topic, however, I think the book won’t likely teach you anything new. In the end, I don’t think it goes far enough.

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

I wouldn’t have thought of “Red and the Wolves” as a retelling of “Little Red Riding Hood” if it wasn’t tagged that way. Red and Grand Mother live alone in a post-apocalyptic forest. Red hunts down “nawa”, creatures of supposed corruption, that she then returns to Grand Mother for exorcism. Then Red rescues a wolf-woman, Sil, from a trap that has injured her leg. As Sil recovers in Red’s cabin, they grow closer, leading to discussions of where the humans and other animals have gone. I like the artwork here. The story builds up a bit slowly at times, but I did enjoy it overall.

“Coral and the Devil” is set in Prague of the 1930s. Coral works as a fortune teller in a carnival. She can also see the Devil, who engages her in conversation for reasons that are not entirely clear. Coral’s father was a rabbi know for his ability at exorcisms. And we also have the Nazis taking over parts of Czechoslovakia. The detail on the art is nice here, as is the lettering, and the story is interesting. I give it negative points for presenting Hitler as the handiwork of the Devil, instead of a natural outcome of the system that produced him. A quirky story if you are into supernatural historical stories.
