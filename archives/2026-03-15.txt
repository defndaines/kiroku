Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

-- Short Fiction --

“Autogas Ferryman” is an urban fantasy short story set in Thailand. Our protagonist is a cab driver who has been able to see ghosts since his mother passed. It sometimes leads to odd interactions, like when one living and one non-living passenger get into the cab at the same time. But often, it’s enough to drop them off at their destination. But then he picks up a passenger who has a more complicated need. This is a great story, with some political and social critique as well as humor. I hope it is a contender for awards this year.

In “The Shape of Stones”, a student sets off to southwest Iceland to try to identify a stone used in rituals more than a thousand years ago. She is exploring based upon a brief mention in the sagas. As she catalogs her findings, some unusual things start to happen. I liked this, though it doesn’t wrap things up in a bow for you at the end.

“The Secret Lives of the Nine Negro Teeth of George Washington” won the Nebula and Locus Awards. There is one section per tooth, and this dips into the paranormal. The tales are based upon actual people the first president held enslaved, but imagines that the teeth have some supernatural effect on the wearer. A good story, though I don’t find this kind of alternate history particularly appealing.

The short story “Buffalo” won the Locus Award. It imagines a meeting between H.G. Wells and the author’s father in the 1930s. Wells is near the end of his career, while the father is still getting started. Each approaches science fiction in their own way, with their own goals. I don’t know enough about Wells to tell if the interpretation of him is remotely like the real man. I found the story quite slow and uninteresting, and if I had to guess, this won an award because of the portrayal of Wells. Not recommended.

“Bears Discover Fire” won the Hugo, Locus, and Nebula Awards for short story. This is a fun and quirky story, which imagines that bears have learned to use fire. They are gathering, particular in the highway medians near Bowling Green, Kentucky. The narrator is also facing his mother’s waning days in a home. Definitely recommended.

-- Nonfiction --

I finally got around to reading “How to Blow Up a Pipeline”, and I’d like to catch the film at some point. The author examines and confronts the fetishization of non-violence, particularly in the environmental movement. He does an excellent job of looking at the numerous examples held up as non-violent which in fact were not. With capitalism’s intent to fully destroy the environment in the name of profit, he carves out space for the kinds of actions that the movement should consider. It’s easy to imagine this transferring to other struggles: like if as soon as word got out that the government bought a warehouse for immigrant detention, someone burned that to the ground? Recommended for all, even if just to clarify your own stance.

-- Manga --

I rewatched “Akira” recently, and figured it was time to read by original manga, since I’d heard it was different. It’s kinda weird to read it, with some scenes matching to the movie quite closely, but then other plot divergences are surprising. The gang fighting, for example, makes a bit more sense in the manga. And there’s a lot more drug use that didn’t make it on screen. I’m looking forward to reading the rest.

I got most of the volumes of “Frieren: Beyond Journey’s End” from the library, so this week has been devoted to working through those. It’s a great story, with just a bit more context compared to the anime. I’m just about caught up reading to season one of the anime. I’m sure I’ll get ahead of season two this week.

-- Graphic Novels --

“The Genie of Beirut” is a fictionalized portrayal of a neighborhood in Beirut in the early 1970s, sitting on the border between the majority Muslim and majority Christian sections. The neighborhood is quite diverse, but you see it all break down as the civil war takes hold. I feel like most of my reading on Lebanon has concentrated on later in war, though I cannot say I was surprised by anything I read here. It’s a lament for what was lost, but is focused on the personal and doesn’t provide much context beyond growing ethnic divisions.

“Lebanon Is Burning” collects several comic works written by Yazan Al-Saadi over the past decade or so. Comics cover Kuwait, Lebanon, Bahrain, Syria, Egypt, Palestine, Canada, Sudan, and Yemen. The actual comics are 6–10 pages apiece, but each includes a preface from the author, information about the artist, and a page-by-page break down of some aspects of the comics the average reader wouldn’t know. With the prefaces, it reads a little slow, but the breadth of information and experiences here is worth reading.
