## Fiction

I’d been meaning to read “The Goblin Emperor” for a while, based upon multiple positive reviews. Maia is the half-Goblin son of the Elven emperor, a fourth son and product of a diplomatic marriage, who is sent off to the hinterlands. But then the emperor and the other sons die when their airship crashes, so Maia is thrust into leadership. This book can sometimes feel like not much is happening, since so much of it is the descriptions and playing out of court intrigue. Although I’m generally less interested in a main character who has this kind of power, I quickly found myself rooting for Maia and eager to read. Recommended.

## Short Fiction

I cannot remember why I put a hold in for “Dead Weight”, but I’m glad I did. This Icelandic novella is quick, and you know from the preface where things are likely to land. Unnur, in her thirties and working a corporate job, comes home to find a black cat in her house. The next day, the cat is waiting outside, leading to an encounter with the cat’s owner, Ásta. But both women are in less than ideal relationships. I wasn’t surprised by how things turned out, but it was a fun ride and totally worth picking up.

I picked up “In the Halls of the Makeshift King” because it was a 2026 Locus Award Short Story finalist. I’ve read a couple of Buckell’s other short stories. Imagine a world where pilgrims have traveled to Conan’s Wheel of Pain, but much bigger and slower moving and requiring much more labor. And then once a year someone is elected to see which way the wheel will turn. From the beginning, the story reads like allegory. It’s a story of coming to terms with loss, but I wouldn’t really recommend it to anyone.

I’ll occasionally read some short stories when they come out, and “The Potential Side Effects of Roleplay Stimulation Therapy” caught my eye with “roleplay”. Emma was a violin prodigy, but then that all came to an end after a crash breaks her fingers. She is in therapy to see if she can recover movement in her hand. Then she gets to know another young woman trying roleplay stimulation therapy for entirely different reasons. This story is written in second person. It unfolds a bit mysteriously. The sci-fi element is light and doesn’t really shape the story. I was nonplussed by the tale.

“From Across Time” won the 2025 Nommo Award for Short Story. Set in a future Nigeria, Adaeze replays a video as she works. Time travel is real, and the woman in the video had been in a serious relationship with Adaeze on a different timeline, but that has now never come to be. The conception and structure of this story is quite cool. It definitely triggered different thoughts for me around the ideas. While not amazing, this was worth the read.

## Nonfiction

Silky Shah is now Executive Director of Detention Watch Network, and “Unbuild Walls” is a mix of the history of immigration activism over the last quarter century along with an analysis of what has worked and what hasn’t along the way. The book shows the intimate connection between the U.S.’s current lock-them-up and ship-them-off approach to the whole carceral system that has become the U.S. “solution” to crime since the ’70s and ’80s. Spoiler alert, it’s the same system, and abolition needs to be the response to the whole mess. There are good insights here, particularly into understanding counter-productive approaches to the problem. Definitely recommend.

## Manga

I tore through all the “Chainsaw Man” books back in 2024, but then haven’t remembered to keep up since then. It took me a bit to catch back up with what was happenings, with the aid of some online resources to remember character details. These three books end one arc and start another, and renewed my interest. Another volume just came out, but not sure when I’ll get my hands on it.

## Graphic Novels

Given that this was one of the most banned books, it was probably inevitable that I’d get around to reading “Gender Queer”. I read the annotated version which recently became available. I have to admit I skimmed some of the annotations after a while, but I read most of them, and they were a nice context to add around the story as well as updating on events over the last eight year. This is a moving story, about Maia’s experience growing up and coming into eir identity. I can see why this is so highly rated, touching on many topics and feeling even-handed and generous along the way.

“The Seasons” is one of those books I could have easily forgotten to continue with, but then the library got volume two only a week after volume one. The adventurous tale continues, with some reveal about what happened to the parents a decade ago. This does feel like a rather thin allegory for the role phones are playing in our society. I would be willing to continue when the next volume comes out.

A graphic novel written by an Ecuadorian woman framed around bicycles checked off enough boxes to give a try. “All My Bicycles” gives brief glimpses into Paola’s life, often more meditative than substantial. It also takes place across several countries, including Colombia and Argentina. It’s not a particular compelling book, but I did like it.
