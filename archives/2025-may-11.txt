11 May Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“Water Moon” is the story of a pawn-shop owner, where the items traded in are not exchanged for money. The tale starts in Japan, but also exists in a fantastical realm heavily influenced by Japanese folklore. The male character is a physicist, and I often had the feeling that if I knew more about theoretical physics, many of the events were meant to map to those ideas. In a fantasy book, I was jarred early on by the notion that someone raised in Japan until the age of 9 would NOT know the story of Urashima Tarō. Not recommended.

“You Dreamed of Empires”, translated from Spanish, re-imagines the 1519 encounter of Hernán Cortés and Moctezuma. As I read, I wondered how much it deviated from the actual historical events (except in an obvious way that’s a plot point). The story is humorous at times, and breaks the fourth wall a couple times for interesting effect. I also felt like I learned a lot about the conditions in Tenochtitlan five hundred years ago. I’m still processing the novel and looking forward to the book club discussion later this month.

“Someone You Can Build a Nest In” is my fourth book among the Hugo finalists this year. This is a delightful fantasy tale, told from the perspective of a “monster”. Would this qualify as “romantasy”, since it is, at its core, an enemies to lovers story? (I didn’t, honestly, know this about the book going in, but that is all given away in the blurb.) Of the Hugo novels so far, I would place this second. Not destined to be a classic of the genre, but enjoyable for fantasy fans.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

I stumbled upon the Nommo Awards recently, which recognize speculative fiction from Africa. “Iyanu: Child of Wonder”, Volume 2, won for graphic novel in 2022, and the first two volumes are on Comics Plus. An animated series is also out, though I’m good with just reading the books. It is a chosen one tale, but I still found the story compelling. The Yoruba-inspired world is a joy to explore. I’ll try to read volumes 3 and 4 when I get a chance.

“The Savage Sword of Conan” volume 8 wasn’t the best, but still enjoyable. The first story is the most fun, and the artwork reminded me a bit of early TMNT.
