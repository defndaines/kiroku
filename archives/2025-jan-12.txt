12 January Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“Headshot” hit my radar last year because it was on the longlist for the Booker. It delves into past and future lives of eight girls competing in a young women’s boxing tournament in Reno, Nevada. I found myself choosing sides as I learned about each girl, not based on ability but based upon backstory. In the end, I found the book just OK.

-- Nonfiction --

“Soldiers and Kings: Survival and Hope in the World of Human Smuggling” won the National Book Award last year for nonfiction. It’s an excellent dive into the lives of the people who work as guides for migrants crossing into Mexico, focusing on several Hondurans. De León started his research around 2015, and the book covers events through and after the pandemic. We see the changes to smuggling, as the U.S. ramps up its efforts against migrants (Biden deported a record number last year) through support for foreign militaries, empowering gangs and mafias along the way. There’s also a powerful scene about Honduran healthcare, where we see what it is like to visit a hospital in the second poorest country in the Western Hemisphere. In an interview I listened to after reading the book, the author also points out that Western Union makes over a million dollars a day from the smuggling trade.

“The Wide Wide Sea: Imperial Ambition, First Contact and the Fateful Final Voyage of Captain James Cook” is a sympathetic history of Cook’s final voyage, starting in July 1776. It is adventurous and gave me a sense of European contact with people of the Pacific. Recommended if that’s something you would like to learn more about, though I felt that there was a tension with the author persuading the reader that Cook wasn’t as bad as he is sometimes portrayed.

“The United States Governed by Six Hundred Thousand Despots: A True Story of Slavery; A Rediscovered Narrative, with a Full Biography” is the slave narrative of John Jacobs, the brother of the author of “Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl”. It was published in 1855 in an Australian paper, so was not censored the way American-publishes narratives were. The text was essentially lost for a century and a half, until the editor found it while trying to research Harriet Jacobs’ son, who joined his uncle in Australia. I did not read the entire book, which includes the editor’s research as well, but this is a significant work worth reading for history buffs.

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“We Sing From the Heart: How The Slants® Took Their Fight for Free Speech to the Supreme Court” is a picture book which I got from the library thinking it was a graphic novel. It covers the history of Simon Tam’s fight to copyright the all Asian-American band’s name. I knew this history already, but it is a good overview if you don’t know it. The material feels a little advanced for a picture book, but I did like the blend of art, history, and lyrics.
