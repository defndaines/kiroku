## Fiction

I got a used copy of “The Night Circus” a while ago, but it took book club to force my hand. It was nominated for a lot of awards, but only won the Locus for First Novel. From a young age, Celia and Marco are set against each other in a magic competition. Celia has innate talent; Marco learns everything from books. When they are old enough, Le Cirque des Rêves is created as the battleground. Neither of them really knows what the goal is or how a winner is declared. So much of this book is about the vibes, the well wrought descriptions of the circus that sounds like a true delight if you are unaware of the underlying truth. Where the book fell flat for me was the romance, which came out of nowhere and felt nothingburger, not to mention the horrible way Isobel is treated.

## Short Fiction

I came across “Agni” because it was a 2025 Ignyte Award finalist for Short Story. Ashanti and other girls have been raised in the temple from a young age. Each of them must show that they can withstand the sacred fire; their ability to hold the fire and not burn evidence of their devotion. This was a great story set in an India with only a touch of fantasy.

I won an ebook of “A Girl Made of Time” via a Goodreads giveaway. Carys’ family is struggling to survive the famine, when a path of glowing mushrooms opens up to her. Abandoning her collapsed mother, she meets a future version of herself. There is an interesting concept here, with three timelines overlapping at once. But then there are elements which are just odd and jarring, like one version of herself placing caches of prepared food in odd places for her younger self to find. The characters also felt modern, and I wasn’t sure how intentional this was.

## Nonfiction

“Here Where We Live Is Our Country” mixes the author’s research into her grandfather with a history of eastern European Jews and the Bund. The Bund’s history is important for many reasons: their focus on solidarity and “hereness” (doikayt), their role in resisting fascism, and their early and emphatic critique of Zionism as a deadly trap for working-class Jews. The concept of hereness is relevant to us all and an important stance in opposition to segregationists and racists. This is a mostly excellent history only marred by its obeisance to anti-communism: the focus on Lenin as a person and conveniently skimming some details does call the work into question, but I don’t doubt that the scholarship is otherwise solid.

## Manga

I felt like I’d heard of “Akame ga KILL!” somewhere, so figured it was worth giving the first volume a try. Tatsumi and his two friends set out for the capital to make names for themselves and save their village with the money they’ll earn. But they get separated, and the capital is not what he expected at all. A young heiress takes him in and offers him a place to stay, but then a group of assassins attack the family one night. There are parts of this story that are fun, but the concept felt a bit too contrived for me. It has the boobs and risk of nudity tropes that peg the target audience, which isn’t me.

It had been a while since I read the first volume of “Adabana”, but I had a note that I wanted to continue the series. This is a dark story involving sexual assault, and volume 2 contains salacious depictions of the two female characters which were a disturbing juxtaposition to the events. I did like how the story wrapped up in volume 3, with some justice but also realistic. I’d recommend this, but only if you are OK with what I just described.

## Graphic Novels

Did I start the week thinking I’d read two distinct stories about Jews fighting Nazis? No, but “The Holy Roller” added some humor to a bleaker reading week. Levi abandoned his Ohio town and his pro-bowler father as soon as he could, but word comes in that his father is dying. He returns to find Neo-Nazis terrorizing the town. When they jump him, an artfully deployed bowling ball saves his life. Can the new superhero land a strike for justice? This is a sometimes funny, sometimes brutal, and sometimes silly story that worked for me. The tongue-in-cheek approach holds it together, as you look past the miracle healing Levi seems to undergo between fights.
