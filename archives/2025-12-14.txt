14 December Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

I heard about “The Slip” either when it won the 2025 Kirkus Prize or was shortlisted earlier. We start in 2008, ten years after the disappearance of sixteen-year-old Nathaniel Rothstein, when a note is left on his uncle’s windshield. Then we shift to 1998, when Nathaniel is sent to Austin for the summer after getting into a fight back east. He works at a nursing home under the guidance of a fellow member of boxing gym his uncle trains at. The story bounces between first-person perspectives of a number of characters, unclear at first how they are all related. With two intertwined coming-of-age stories, we don’t even find out what happened to Nathaniel until the last pages. I found all of the characters engaging even when I didn’t like them, and I was comfortable in the confusion of what was going on. The best “literary” book I’ve read in six months.

I requested a hold on “Flesh” after it won the 2025 Booker Prize. The book follows the life of István, a Hungarian who experiences an unexpected sexual awakening at fifteen. The story is punctuated with occasional, unannounced time skips. Many of his pivotal life events are triggered by sexual encounters. Throughout it all, István maintains his adolescent indifferent demeanor, particularly highlighted when another adolescent enters the story. We’re only ever given dates indirectly by reference to “current” events, but I think the protagonist is the same age as myself and the author, so spanning roughly 1989 through today. The writing kept me going, though I never liked the characters or got engaged in the events of István’s life. In the end, the book is a readable “OK”.

“Automatic Noodle” is a fast, cozy read. In San Francisco of the 2060s, four intelligent bots wake up after an unexpected period of disconnection. Still dealing with the trauma of the war between California and America, they  have to scramble to get power and make money to avoid getting enslaved if their contracts aren’t paid. This is tight writing with good world-building. It is easy to empathize with the four main characters, and I was surprised that it was already over when I got to the end.
