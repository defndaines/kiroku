Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

This book collects two stories and a poem, with introductory and concluding scholarly essays. Alexander Bogdanov was a founding member of the Bolshevik faction, though between the first and second book he was expelled. “Red Star” (1908) portrays an activist, Leonid, who is recruited to travel to Mars in the aftermath of the 1905 revolution. Martians look mostly like humans, but their society transitioned to socialism more than a hundred years ago. On Mars, he sees the advances they’ve made alongside the problems they still face (some ecological). This story was enjoyable, with some nice social commentary mixed in. I would recommend it to fans of early sci-fi. “Engineer Menni” (1913) takes place 150 years earlier, exploring the shift on Mars from feudalism to capitalism to socialism, presumably translated by the protagonist of the first book. This story is much drier, with large segments of conversations and some plot elements that are a bit hard to parse. The ending descends into a mess that I had trouble making sense of. I do not recommend this second story at all, so would probably recommend the 2025 stand-alone translation if you want to read Bogdanov’s fiction.

-- Nonfiction --

“You Can’t Kill a Man” follows the life of Angelo Herndon, who moves to Georgia in the 1930s to support the unification of black and white workers in fighting for better relief payments during the Depression. He is a Communist Party activist, and when he is arrested, this is used to claim that his support of the “Black Belt” is a call for violent insurrection, using a 19th century law that carries a potential death sentence. The case goes all the way to the Supreme Court, twice, and marks a historical shift in “free speech” in the United States. I was reminded of the Niemöller poem, “First They Came”, and core role communists and labor activists have played in acquiring the rights Americans enjoyed in the late 20th century. For the majority of U.S. history, there was no enforced “freedom of speech”. The 1st amendment starts “Congress shall make no law …”, which was interpreted to mean that it was just fine for states and local governments to do so. Recommended for folks interested in American legal history with context.

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“Hakim’s Odyssey” is a three-part series that follows the life of Hakim, a young man running a successful plant nursery when the Syrian civil war breaks out in 2011. It is framed by the artist visiting with Hakim, who now (2018) lives as a refugee with this family in Aix-en-Provence. Hakim is the eldest son, living in a Damascus suburb, looking forward to life and not particularly interested in politics. But the war quickly intrudes on the family, and after getting kidnapped by the government and losing his home and business, the family decides it would be safer if he left the country until things calm down. But life as a Syrian in Lebanon, Jordan, and Turkey is not easy. The artist captures the story well, and I am looking forward to finishing the trilogy.
