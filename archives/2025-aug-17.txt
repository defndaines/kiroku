17 August Two-week Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“The Maid and the Crocodile” was our first road-trip audiobook, and the narration is excellent. An African-inspired fantasy world with a heavy dose of real-world influence. Small Sade has magical abilities that allow her to clean more than just houses. She has been an orphan for over a decade when the story starts, and the author does a great job of realistically portraying someone who has lived through real hardship and values their independence. It takes place in the author’s Raybearer world, but I didn’t feel like I was missing anything for not having read those books first, though I do plan to read them now.

I read “The Golden Compass” with a book club at work over twenty years ago. I’ve heard several booktubers I like mention how influential it was on them, which inspired me to reread and also introduce the rest or the family to the story (though we’ve seen a forgettable movie at some point). Lyra has grown up an orphan ward of Oxford University, but this is an alternate universe where all humans have a “daemon” manifestation of their “soul”. When the Gobblers kidnap her closest friend, she is thrust into an adventure to find him. I cannot remember if I finished these series back then, but I plan to continue on when I can fit the rest of the trilogy in.

“Wolf Hall” is a beast of a book that took me over two weeks to read, and not just because of vacation. This award-winning historical fiction follows the life of Thomas Cromwell, as his influence grows under the reign of King Henry VIII. Although I tend to be less interested in the lives of the wealthy and powerful, the book does a great job of pulling you in. You get a great sense of what it was like to live in the early 1500s in England. We explore the English Reformation, with the shifting relationship to the Catholic Church as Henry seeks to invalidate his first marriage. The sequels are well regarded, so I can see myself picking them up in the future.

I bought “The Flat Woman” recently after reading an intriguing review. It’s a rather bizarre dystopian story, where the main character’s “boundaries” are not quite fixed when she is under stress. Corporate ash is killing off birds and polluting everything, but obviously women are to blame! Early on, the protagonist’s mother is arrested for “killing” hundreds of birds in a “terrorist” act. This is not an easy read, especially the ending, but it is a biting critique of the climate crisis.

-- Nonfiction --

The local bookstore kicked off its activist book club with “Mutual Aid”, although I wasn’t able to attend. This is a mostly straightforward book, explaining what “mutual aid” is, especially in contrast to charity. From my reading, what distinguishes mutual aid is that is also demands transformational change while alleviating the worsening conditions of capitalism. Much of the book focuses on tactics so that the reader can participate in making the world a better place. I did find some examples confusing, exposing potential contradictions in the definitions. The book doesn’t get too deep into large-scale changes, and when it did I could see the cracks. But overall, it feels like a useful books for young activists.
