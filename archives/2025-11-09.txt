9 November Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

I don’t remember how I found out about “The Diary of Curious Cuthbert”, but it’s been on the shelf for years. It’s a fairly short book of poetry aimed at a younger audience, but touching on scientific themes in a fantasy setting. The stories progress to the conclusion identified at the beginning. I’d recommend this for late elementary school students.

I skimmed Faulkner in high school to write a paper, but this was my first time really engaging with his work. “Light in August” is set at the time of its release, 1932, with frequent digressions into the history of the different characters. Lena Grove is about to give birth and ends up in Jefferson, Mississippi, searching for the father of her child. Joe Christmas has been living in Jefferson for three years and transitioned from a mill worker to town bootlegger. All of the characters are flawed, and most of them are not sympathetic. We explore the small-mindedness of the South, along with the daily effort it takes to maintain racism. The “n” word is used A LOT, reflecting the racism of the different characters. Though we should be honest that the author’s voice evinces some of that racism, too, while pointing out the cost of standing against it. I can understand the appeal of his writing (Faulkner won the Nobel in Literature), but I doubt I’ll read him again.

“The Shining” is my second Stephen King horror, and I found it executed better, although I like the concept behind “Pet Sematary” more. Jack Torrance takes a job as winter caretaker of the Overlook Hotel, after getting fired from his previous job as a teacher. It seems like a simple job, the main caveat being that he, his wife, and son will be snowed in at the hotel all winter. But Jack is a recovering alcoholic with violent tendencies, his five-year-old son Danny has some kind of telepathic ability, and, oh yeah, a previous caretaker got cabin fever and killed his entire family a few seasons ago. This is a good thriller, only slightly marred by the Magical Negro trope. I’m looking forward to rewatching the movie.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“Heartcore” explores the author’s love life, from high school into adulthood. Much of it will feel relatable to all, though it does at times dip into the specifics of coming of age in the Czech Republic of the ’90s. The art style goes well with the writing and story flow. There is plenty of room for introspection and reflection as the artist looks back on her own experience. I liked the story and would recommend it to others.

“The Summer Hikaru Died” is heating up as the different groups seek to solve the supernatural infestation that plagues their community. I’m still quite engaged in the story and will definitely follow it to its conclusion.

I’m a long-time fan of Kuper’s art; I own a volume he signed for me over twenty years ago. “Wish We Weren’t Here” is a collection of wordless four-panel pieces he did for Charlie Hebdo over the last several years. The focus is entirely on the destruction that capitalism has brought to bear against the environment. While there are some excellent comics here, it also does get repetitive.
