28 September Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“On the Origin of Species and Other Stories” is a collection of seven stories, translated from Korean. I bought the book over two years ago, but then saw a review of it recently that inspired me to pick it up. These are excellent stories. Several of them start with a seemingly straightforward premise that ends up turning your assumptions on their head. Definitely recommend the collection for sci-fi fans, with tales ranging from shutting down an online video game to a far future where robots are the dominant species.

“Home” is a short story, 4.5 in the sequence of Murderbot tales. At only nineteen pages and this deep into the series, it’s hard to say anything much about it. Unlike “Rapport”, which was a side story about non-SecUnit characters from the second book, this one includes Murderbot, but is told from Dr. Mensah’s perspective. My hold for book five, “Network Effect”, just came in, so I‘ll be reading that next week.

-- Nonfiction --

I reread “If We Burn” to prepare for the book club in a couple weeks, as I’m moderating and wanted to get it fresh in my head after two years. It’s an excellent overview of the popular uprisings that marked the 2010s, with a focus on what worked and what didn’t. The author was a journalist in Brazil for most of that decade, so his coverage there is the strongest. He touches on nine other countries, including “Arab Spring” events. In nearly ever case, “horizontalist” protests ended in worse conditions for the country. Highly recommended for students of history or contemporary politics, and those who hope to make change in our world.

“How Europe Underdeveloped Africa” is a broad historical overview of, well ... the title. He starts with level setting the state of Africa before Europe began global expansion in the 1500s. We see the way the slave trade warped and diminished the people of Africa. We then work our way forward to the colonial period, which started in the 1800s, and deep dive into the numerous ways the European powers (and the U.S.) actively worked to keep the various countries held back economically. This was written in 1971, so it wraps up with the state of things at independence as it was won after WWII. It’s a bit dry and dense at times, but this is what I’d recommend to anyone who wants to get started understanding African history.

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“Traces of Madness” is a memoir of a man who started hearing voices and sounds after a psychotic break at age nineteen. He is thirty-five at the time of writing. He talks about his experiences, including trying to get a diagnosis, dealing with different medications, and just trying to form friendships with a condition that is not going to go away. It’s a touching and informative read.
