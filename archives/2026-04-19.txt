## Fiction

I grabbed “Four Hands” off the shelf on occasion of my trip to Mexico, not that it took place where I was visiting, but to read a Mexican author while there. (I’ll make this a habit for future trips abroad.) This story, written in 1989, jumps around time periods, characters, and locations from chapter to chapter. We have chapters where Stan Laurel is in Mexico. We have two journalist, a Mexican and an American, who cover the politics of Latin America. We have a CIA operation out of NYC whose goals only become clear late into the book. We have a drug dealer and his associates. Dropping cultural and literary references along the way. I enjoyed the book, but because of how slowly the story unfolds, I could see if frustrating some readers. I could see myself picking up another Taibo book in the future.

“She Who Became the Sun” takes place through the mid-1300s, re-imagining the collapse of the Yuan dynasty. The protagonist is orphaned then assumes her brother’s identify after he passes. It’s a little Mulan-coded, but the story does not get weighed down by that or other tropes about a woman growing up passing as a man. Zhu is determined to be “great”, and for her this means playing a role in bringing about the Mongols’ downfall. This is enjoyable historical fiction, though I wouldn’t say I loved it. There is a second book in the series, and I don’t intend to read it. One complain is the book is tagged as “fantasy”, but the small fantastical elements are nearly irrelevant: you could skip a couple pages and not even realize it is there. So, if you decide to read this, do it because it is historical fiction.

## Nonfiction

Imagine a friend casually walking you through the ins and outs of the Erlang ecosystem and you have “The BEAM Book”. This is ultimately a reference book, but written such that you can read it front to back instead of skipping around just to what you need. As a reference book, it does get a little dry at times, but it’s a great resource for Erlang and Elixir developers to get to know the nitty gritty of it all.

“Motherland” is hard to review: there’s some excellent material here, alongside a few sections I could have skipped, and then parts where the author’s biases are laid bare. On the plus side, some of the biased writing will likely inspire me to read up more on those areas later. It is an interesting study, when you go from the quickest and most impactful advance in women’s rights—probably ever—immediately following 1917, to the Russia of today where women’s subservience and obsession with fashion and appearance seems the polar opposite. The transition from the horrors of Stalin in the 1930s to the essential role women played in defeating the Nazis was hard to grasp from the text. I definitely recommend “The Unwomanly Face of War” for those interested in that topic. One gem of the book is the quote from the last two decades where it is openly admitted that the decriminalization of domestic violence is to give men a way to vent down instead of up against the system. I would only recommend this book if you already have a progressive grounding in 20th century history.

## Manhwa

I picked up “Overgeared” to try out a manhwa that might be up my alley. This story takes place primarily in a fantasy game world, but with occasional peaks into the main character’s real life as a 20-something putz. The opening section uses in media res, but then clearly gives away that the main character will become the greatest blacksmith in the game. We then jump back to his origins. There’s a lot of explanation of game mechanics. Overall, the story is fun and not too cringe, but I won’t be continuing on.

## Manga

Finally got the latest volume of “Frieren: Beyond Journey’s End” from the library. There’s not much I can say about the plot this deep into the series, but still going strong. Cannot wait for the next volume, and still excited for the anime even if it is way behind the manga.

The most surprising thing about Volume 2 of “Akira” is that the end is also nearly the end of the movie. There are four more volumes after this, so everything from here will be virgin territory. I felt that this volume was closer to the anime than Volume 1, which is likely an indication that the story was coming together better for the author by this point. Looking forward to continuing the series.
