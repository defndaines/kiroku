Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“We Will Rise Again” is an anthology of fiction short stories and nonfiction interviews and essays, with an eye toward resistance and more positive futures. Several of the works give pause for reflection, and several are bound to spark ideas or new ways of thinking about old problems. All of the pieces are good, and some are quite excellent. Definitely recommended.

I heard about “On the Calculation of Volume” a lot last year and was finally inspired to request the ebooks from the library. There are seven volumes planned, as I understand it, but only the first three are out in English so far. Tara Selter, an antiquarian bookseller from northern France, has gotten stuck in 18 November. In this take on the time loop concept, she wakes wherever she went to sleep, allowing her to return from a trip to Paris to her husband. And some things stay with her from day to day. This first volume dives deep into the emotional journey of being stuck in time, unsure of how to break out. I quite enjoyed this book and will continue on with the series.

I grabbed a copy of “The Destructors” after starting a re-watch of “Donnie Darko”. It’s a quick tale, set in post-WWII London. The boys in the neighborhood decide to take apart “Old Misery”’s house, which survived the Blitz intact. It’s a quick read, and nice bonus context for the movie. I’m reminded that I need to read more Graham Greene.

“The Empusium” is my second book by Nobel laureate Olga Tokarczuk. In 1913, Mieczysław leaves Lwów (now Lviv) for a tuberculosis sanitarium in Görbersdorf (now Sokołowsko). Housed with several other men, all but one older than him, the “cure” mostly involves regular walks in the mountain air. The men “philosophize” on many things and, absent any women, their favorite topic is women, but not in a flattering way. There’s also something suspicious that happens every November. This is a slow book, and most of the characters are not sympathetic. The epilogue points out that many of the arguments the men make are actual quotes from a number of famous men. The book is also an homage/rejoinder to “The Magic Mountain” by Thomas Mann, which I haven’t read, though would like to at some point. I didn’t like this as much as “Drive Your Plow”, but would still recommend to folks with a taste for headier literary works.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

I finished books two and three of “Hakim’s Odyssey”, after reading book one last week. In Türkiye, Hakim struggles to make enough money to live on, but does meet Najmeh. Eventually prospects in Türkiye darken, and he decides to risk the sea to get to Greece. In the final book, Hakim makes his way through eastern Europe. The last book ended up being the most powerful to me, with its mix of humane encounters alongside xenophobic hatred. At the end, we get an update about Hakim and his family’s current status (as of the late 2010s). The series does an excellent job of putting a human face to the plight of refugees. Definitely recommended.
