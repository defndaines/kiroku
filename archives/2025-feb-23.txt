23 February Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“We Do Not Part” is a companion novel to “Human Acts”, and while not strictly necessary, I recommend reading that book first. This is my fifth book by Han Kang, who won the Nobel Prize last year. This novel explores the legacy of the Jeju Uprising that began in 1948 and ended in a (U.S.-backed) bloodbath. This is by no means an easy book to read, emotionally, but highly recommended. The first part was rather disjoint, and feels like the author working through the trauma of having researched and written the book about Gwangju.

“Alien Clay” is my fourth book by Adrian Tchaikovsky, and it is definitely the best so far. (I haven’t read the “Children of Time” series yet.) It starts off exciting, with our narrator getting violently dumped on the planet “Kiln”, a prison labor camp. He’s a professor who organized against the oppressive regime back on Earth, but he’s also trained in a specialty that should prove useful as humanity explores a new planet and new life forms. I think this should be up for the Hugo, and if so, it gets my vote.

-- Nonfiction --

“No Right to an Honest Living: The Struggles of Boston’s Black Workers in the Civil War Era” won the Pulitzer Prize in History last year. It is fairly readable for what feels like an academic work. The book thoroughly explores the lives of black workers in Boston from the 1850s, through the Civil War, and until the end of Reconstruction. We see the hypocrisy of abolitionists who seem to think freedom from slavery is enough, but still want to maintain a social hierarchy. We also see the way Irish immigrants were pitted against black workers in the city.
