21 September Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“There Are Rivers in the Sky”, Elif Shafak | 2024 | Türkiye, U.K., France | 4.5 | audio | 464 | literary, historical, contemporary |

“There Are Rivers in the Sky” starts with the last days of the Assyrian king Ashurbanipal (631 BCE). We then jump to the birth of Arthur Smith along the Thames in the 1840s. In 2014, a nine-year-old Yazidi girl in Türkiye is going deaf as her village is being eliminated by the government to build a dam. The final protagonist is a contemporary woman of Turkish descent in London, a hydrologists whose marriage is failing. The novel weaves together these tales, all connected by water and rivers (Tigris and Thames). It weaves together the “Epic of Gilgamesh” and its translation with two different genocides, and keeps you engaged the whole time. I could have done without the water memory bits, but this is still a book I can easily recommend.

“You Weren’t Meant to Be Human”, Andrew Joseph White | 2025 | U.S. | 3.5 | audio | 336 | horror, sci-fi, fantasy, LGBT, queer |

“You Weren’t Meant to Be Human” is the latest release from Andrew Joseph White, and the third book of his I’ve read. Crane is a mute, trans man with autism in his early twenties who lives in a depopulated part of West Virginia. There is a hive of insects and larvae which must be fed human remains and directs the actions of the group. And then Crane gets pregnant. This horror book is all the trigger warnings, from body horror to self harm. While White is an excellent author who writes believable and propulsive prose, I don’t know who I’d ever recommend this book to. Definitely pick up his other books, though.

| Universality | Natasha Brown | 2025 | U.K. | 4.0 | audio | 152 | novella, literary, thriller, mystery, contemporary |

I learned about “Universality” from the Booker longlist, and it was a finalist for the Orwell Prize. It’s a skewering of journalism and mainstream notions of “class” in the U.K. It also mocks the way white racists use the “diversity” shibboleth to pretend advocacy for the “working man”. I did not go into the book aware of this, so it took me a bit to catch on, and I suspect this may be the reason for its poor ratings online. I think I may not have appreciated this as much if I hadn’t recently watched “Lost Illusions”, a 2021 French film of Balzac’s 1840s work skewering journalism and class. Warning, there are no likable characters in the book.

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“Whatever Happened to Frankie King”, Jay Neugeboren | 2024 | U.S. | 4.0 | ebook | 119 | nonfiction, biography |

“Whatever Happened to Frankie King” is a graphic biography of a man who set records in high school in NYC for basketball, was set to play in college, then abruptly quit and fell off the radar. Piecing together King’s life from articles and interviews with his family (two brothers and their children), friends, and admirers, we get the picture of a man who wouldn’t stand by when he saw injustice. He made a living writing fiction, mostly under a pen name. He appears to have struggled with an undefined mental health issue. This book honors the man well.

“The Savage Sword of Conan #10”, Jim Zub | 2025 | U.S. | 4.5 | print | 64 | fantasy, adventure, sword and sorcery |

The latest “The Savage Sword of Conan” includes a tale that wraps up (?) the tale from the monthly comics where Conan and Bělit were betrayed. But a chance encounter with the betrayer ten years later turns out to be something more. There’s also an odd but enjoyable pre-historic tale, as well as my first encounter with a Steve Costigan tale. The cover art is for this last story. A good issue overall.

“A Witch’s Life in Mongol, Vol. 1”, Tomato Soup | 2022 | Japan | 4.0 | ebook | 189 | manga, historical |

The Tezuka Osamu-inspired art of “A Witch’s Life in Mongol” caught my attention, along with the “witch” and “Mongol” bits, of course. This is the first part of a historical fiction story that follows a young slave girl in Persia. This is set-up, with the story only arriving in Mongolia at the tail end of this volume. The historical details appear to be researched, though the dialogue and personalities don’t feel accurate or believable at times. I have no idea where the “witch” part comes in yet. I’m interested enough that I will continue the series when further volumes become available (five have been published in Japanese so far). There’s also an anime in the works by the same studio doing “Dan-da-dan”.

“Nuking Alaska: Notes of an Atomic Fugitive”, Peter Dunlap-Shohl | 2023 | U.S. | 4.5 | ebook | 91 | nonfiction, history, politics, war |

“Nuking Alaska” is a nonfiction graphic novel and part memoir about Alaska’s history with nuclear weapons. It includes details about what happened at the three nuclear missile sites around Anchorage after the second largest recorded earthquake in human history hit in 1964. It also tracks the efforts of the nutjob who inspired Dr. Strangelove to use five nuclear weapons to “build” a harbor near Anchorage. And then there were the three nuclear “tests” in the Aleutians. “November 6, 1971, was the day I lost my faith in the comforting certainty that the adults in charge know what they are doing.” A powerful and fairly short graphic novel I definitely recommend.

“Adabana, Vol. 1”, NoN | 2020 | Japan | 4.0 | ebook | 196 | manga, thriller, mystery, horror, crime |

The first of three volumes of “Adabana” is now available in English. It is a contemporary murder mystery, where a high-school girl confesses to killing her only friend. But then we start flashing back to what happened leading up to that. Her court-appointed lawyers start digging into the case more than the police seem willing to, and nothing is quite what it seems. It’s a disturbing tale that I will follow up on when the rest of it is published in English.
