22 March Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

I picked up “The Decagon House Murders” because it takes place in Ōita Prefecture, and I was searching for books that take place in Kyūshū. Seven college students are in charge of their college mystery club, and they are spending a week on an isolated island which was the site of several murders six months earlier. We know from the very start that someone plans to murder them, but how and why? The book was published in 1987, so technology is relevant to that time. It’s a pretty fun mystery, with the perpetrator walking us through what happened at the end.

“The Memory Police” is a Kafkaesque novel about an island where over time more and more things are removed from nearly everyone’s memory. It never explains the mechanism (that’s not the point). We get the perspective a writer as she comes to terms with successive removals. I found it thought provoking, and contemplative novel about control and memory and what it means to be human. This won‘t be for everyone, but I enjoyed it.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“The Last Halloween 1: Children” follows 10-year-old Mona, stuck at home on her favorite holiday. That is, until a monster attacks. We are then introduced to a world where monsters and undead exist, but the actions of one individual have plummeted the world into chaos. The art is fun, and the characters are quirky. I enjoyed parts of the story, but it also got tedious a points. I won’t be reading further volumes.

“The Savage Sword of Conan” is anchored with a tale from Roy Thomas, which is in turn a reworking of a story by Kipling. The art and action were quite enjoyable, and exactly what I expect from these books.

“Birdking” volume three continues the tale as our hero, Bianca, is now in Atlas. She gets a chance to do some blacksmithing and research, which at first makes her happy. I’m still enjoying the tale, though I found this volume less engaging than the first two. I’ll be back for volume four when it is released.
