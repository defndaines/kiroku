22 December Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“The Wall” is a 1963 post-apocalyptic novel by Austria author Marlen Haushofer. A middle-age women is stranded alone at a hunting lodge when a mysterious, invisible wall blocks off all access to the outside world. From what she can see, everyone on the other side of the wall has died. From here, the sci-fi element recedes into the background, and it is a survival story. The tale unfolds slowly, building tension as the narrator recounts the last two-and-a-half years. Perhaps the first book in this genre written by a woman, I definitely recommend this one if you can handle the pacing.

“Fatelessness” is autofiction about the experience of a 15-year-old Jewish boy taken from his home in Budapest to the concentration camps in 1944. Imre Kertész is Hungary’s only winner of the Nobel Prize in Literature, and this is his most famous book. Any book on the Holocaust can be hard to read, but this one is all the more difficult because of the naïveté of the narrator. It isn’t until about 40% into the book that the narrator realizes what’s going on. There are some nice insights in a few places, but this wouldn’t be my first recommendation for someone looking to read about the experience of a survivor.

My second Hungarian book (ever and of the week) was “The Bone Fire”. Set in what feels like the early 1990s in an unnamed Eastern European country, a thirteen-year-old orphan is taken home by the maternal grandmother she didn’t know she had. This book is beautifully written, with wonderful descriptions of sights, sounds, and smells that don’t feel forced. It explores the aftermath of the fall of the Soviet Union and its associated regimes. As well, the grandmother provides second-person narratives of events around 1944 and after the war, so it was quite interesting to read this right after the Kertész novel (I liked Bone Fire more). The magical realism elements add a flavor that almost touches on fantasy.

-- Nonfiction --

“From the Land of Green Ghosts: A Burmese Odyssey” is Pascal Khoo Thwe‘s autobiography of growing up in Burma. He is a member of the Padaung tribe, and he is in college in Mandalay at the time of the 1988 events (8888 Uprising). He eventually makes it to the University of Cambridge. His writing is drawn out but beautiful. I learned a lot about the modern history and politics of Myanmar (officially changed from Burma in 1989, after most of the events of this book).

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

“Castle Waiting”, Vol. 1, starts off feeling like a fairy tale retelling, then morphs into a story of its own. The world blends fairy tales, anthropomorphic characters, and real-world history. There’s some good humor mixed into the tales. It’s a long volume (457 pages) which did drag in the middle for me, but I’ll check out volume 2, probably in the new year.
