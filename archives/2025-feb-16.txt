16 February Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“The Sword of Kaigen” is my second book by M.L. Wang, after being blown away by “Blood Over Bright Haven” last year. I came across a print copy in a Canadian book story, so bought it then. This is an alternate fantasy world, where the main characters are basically Japanese (names, dress, language), but in a world a little like Avatar, with fire, water, and air bending (she doesn’t use that word). The story was enjoyable, but not nearly on the level of “Bright Haven”. It made me realize that I have a line with fantasy words, where we get in-world terms for measurements and time, which I could have done without. I think the slog at the beginning was because of this. That said, I still liked the story, but it really defies expectations. It is classified as a stand-alone, but there‘s a lot of material at the end which feels like set-up for another book. I am not sure if she intends to write more in the world.

“The Reformatory” is a fictional story about the same juvenile prison as “The Nickel Boys”, but with a supernatural element (haints). Perspective shifts between the brother and sister, and we get a very tense and real exploration of Jim Crow Florida of the 1950s. Where Whitehead’s story feels more literary, this verges on YA. I fully recommend both tales. I think it’s additionally important to read books like this to remind ourselves of what “great” means when the ruling class wants to make things “great again”.

I picked up “Kusamakura” because it came up as a novel set around Kumamoto, which we plan to visit in the near future. I know I’ve read Natsume Sōseki, but it has been nearly thirty years, so I was also motivated to pick up some classics that I haven’t gotten around to. The writing is beautiful and poetic; haikus play an important role. I was also a bit surprised to see the Russo–Japanese War entering the tale. Overall, though, I found it hard to focus on the story, and I would only recommend it to those serious about studying early modern Japanese literature.

-- Nonfiction --

-- Graphic Novels and Manga --

I couldn’t get “William of Newbury” when it was coming out in single issues, so I requested a pull of the TPB. It is a fun story, set in a 12th-century England but with anthropomorphic animals and very real supernatural hauntings. Although I liked the characters and episodic adventures, I don’t plan to pick up the series. The religious bent was a bit much for my tastes, though it is by no means heavy handed.

“Fatale Compendium” was a beast of a book, collecting the entire run into a more than 600 page book. The dark forces combined with a reworking of the femme fatale trope was quite fun, and I didn’t have trouble keeping with the story as it went from chapter to chapter. And the tentacle-headed Bishop was a wonderfully drawn villain. Definitely some fan service with the gorgeous protagonist, but it didn’t feel overdone.

“Outcast, Vol. 1: A Darkness Surrounds Him” is a supernatural mystery in a contemporary setting. Similar to “William”, there’s a strong religious bent, with a priest as an important supporting character. The characters and story are compelling, but I found myself uninterested in how things were going to turn out, so I won’t be continuing with the other volumes that are out.
