13 April Not-so-Weekly Reading Wrap-up

-- Fiction --

“The Houseguest and Other Stories” is a collection of short stories, all with a creepy horror bent. Several of the stories use the same trope, where an named thing is never described and yet sends the characters’ lives into disarray. Sometimes the unnamed thing feels as simple as a pet, other times perhaps something more supernatural, but we never find out what the thing is, only the effects they have. Individually, the stories were fun to read, but when collected together felt a little repetitive.

“I Hotel” feels like an oral history project, and the reader is left wondering how much from the interconnected stories is fictional versus pulled directly from accounts the author researched. The stories span the years 1968 through 1977, when the residents were forcefully evicted on 4 August. Perspectives include the Filipino American residents, as well as Chinese- and Japanese-American activists. Each novella feels honest and respectful to the different points of view, neither biased for or against the individuals involved. The work maps the growth of "Asian American" as an identity in the late ’60s, and I learned a lot.

“Orbital” is literary sci-fi, depicting 24 hours in the lives of six astronauts aboard a space station. The setting is near future, since one character is the child of a hibakusha, but there’s also mention of an event that hasn’t happened yet. The characters’ thoughts wax philosophical, delving into the meaning of life, earth, climate, and faith. I found the book just OK, with some characters indistinct to me as I think back upon the read. This is the fourth book from 2024’s Booker longlist that I’ve read now (I plan to read one more), and I would place it third or fourth in that list, yet it won the award.

“Dearborn” is a collection of ten short stories set in the Lebanese-American community in Dearborn, Michigan. They range from serious to humorous and are only connected by location and little easter eggs. This is the April book selection for Arab American Heritage Month, and I’m glad I got a chance to read this. Several of the stories do not have closure, but this helped it feel more real in its depiction of the characters’ lives.
